# Positioning Angles

Good positioning is the difference between "another product like X" and "the only option for people like me."

Most products fail to find their angle. They describe features. They list benefits. They sound like everyone else.

This skill finds positioning angles that actually differentiate.

---

## The core job

Given a product and its context, identify **3-5 distinct positioning angles** the product can own.

Each angle should be:

1. **True** - Defensible and based on reality
2. **Different** - Not what competitors claim
3. **Relevant** - Matters to the target audience
4. **Clear** - Can be understood in one sentence

**Output format:** For each angle:

- The angle (one-line positioning statement)
- Why it works (the logic)
- Who it appeals to (specific audience)
- What it's against (implicit or explicit contrast)
- Headline example (how it might appear in copy)

---

## Context needed

Before finding angles, gather:

1. **The product/service** - What is it? What does it do?
2. **The category** - How would the market describe what this is?
3. **Current positioning** - How are they positioning it now (if at all)?
4. **Competitors** - Who else solves this problem? How are they positioned?
5. **Audience** - Who is this for? What do they care about?
6. **Differentiators** - What's actually different about this product? (Features, approach, philosophy, origin, results)
7. **Constraints** - Anything they can't or won't claim?

---

## The angle-finding framework

### Step 1: Identify all possible differentiation vectors

Go through each category and list what's true:

**Product-based:**

- Features competitors don't have
- Features done better
- Unique methodology or approach
- Technology or architecture differences
- Speed, simplicity, quality differences

**Market-based:**

- Underserved segment being targeted
- Use case being specialized for
- Price point differentiation
- Geography or market-specific

**Philosophy-based:**

- Contrarian belief about the industry
- Values that guide decisions
- What you refuse to do
- What you believe others get wrong

**Origin-based:**

- Founder story that matters
- Why this was built
- Personal experience that led to it
- Unique access or expertise

**Results-based:**

- Specific outcomes achieved
- Speed to result
- ROI or savings
- Transformation enabled

### Step 2: Find the "Only" or "Best for"

For each vector, ask:

- Is this something where you can claim "only"? (Only product that...)
- Is this something where you can claim "best for"? (Best for people who...)
- Is this something where you can claim "versus"? (Unlike X, we...)

The goal is to find angles where you have a defensible claim to uniqueness or superiority.

### Step 3: Test for resonance

For each potential angle:

- Would the target audience care about this?
- Does it address a real pain or desire?
- Is it distinctive enough to be memorable?
- Can it be explained simply?

### Step 4: Articulate the angle

Turn each winner into a clear positioning statement:

**Format options:**

- [Product] is the only [category] that [differentiator]
- [Product] is [category] for [specific audience]
- Unlike [common approach], [Product] [differentiator]
- [Product]: [Benefit] without [common downside]
- The [adjective] [category] for [audience]

## The Unique Mechanism

In crowded markets, everyone makes similar promises. "Lose weight." "Make money." "Get more leads."

Promises alone don't differentiate. What differentiates is the **MECHANISM** - the unique HOW behind the promise.

The mechanism answers: "Why will this work when other things I've tried haven't?"

### Why mechanisms matter

**The psychology of failed attempts:**

Your prospect has probably tried to solve this problem before. They've bought courses, hired consultants, downloaded apps, read books. Many of those attempts failed.

When they see your promise, their brain immediately says: "I've heard this before. It didn't work."

The mechanism breaks through that skepticism. It gives them HOPE that this time is different - because the APPROACH is different.

> "You've tried diets before. But you've never tried a diet that works WITH your metabolic type instead of against it. That's the difference."

### The three types of mechanisms

**Type 1: The Existing Mechanism (Named)**

You already have something unique. You just haven't named it or emphasized it.

- Look at what you actually DO differently
- Even small differences can become mechanisms when properly named
- Example: Every copywriter writes headlines, but ONE names their approach "The Fascination Formula" and suddenly it's a mechanism
- **The technique:** Give what you do a proprietary name - "The X Method," "The Y System," "The Z Framework"

**Type 2: The Unspoken Mechanism (Revealed)**

Your competitors have this too, but they don't talk about it. You claim it by speaking first.

- Look at the process, ingredients, or approach common in your industry but never explained
- Example: The classic Schlitz beer campaign - all breweries sterilized bottles with steam, but explaining the process in detail made one brand own "purity"
- **The technique:** Explain the "behind the scenes" that customers don't know about. The one who explains it first owns it.

**Type 3: The Innovated Mechanism (Created)**

You've actually created something new. A genuine innovation in approach.

- Requires actual R&D or methodology development
- Built something competitors can't claim because they don't have it
- Example: A genuinely new algorithm, proprietary process, methodology from original research
- **The technique:** Document, name, and protect the innovation. Make it central to your positioning.

### How to find your mechanism

**Step 1: Map your process**

Write out every step of how you deliver results. Don't skip the "obvious" steps.

- What do you do first?
- What information do you gather?
- What analysis do you perform?
- What's your sequence or framework?
- What do you do that others skip?

**Step 2: Identify the difference**

Compare your process to alternatives:

- What do you do that DIY approaches don't?
- What do you do that competitors skip?
- Where do you invest extra effort?
- What's your counterintuitive step?

**Step 3: Name it**

The name matters. It should sound:

- Proprietary (like you invented it)
- Specific (not generic)
- Memorable (easy to repeat)
- Benefit-oriented (hints at outcome)

**Good names:** "The Profit First Method," "The StoryBrand Framework," "The SPIN Selling System"

**Weak names:** "Our approach," "The comprehensive method," "Our process"

**Step 4: Explain why it works**

The mechanism needs a "reason why." Not just "we do X" but "we do X BECAUSE..."

> "We use the Metabolic Mapping approach BECAUSE your body burns fat differently based on your unique hormone profile. Generic diets ignore this, which is why they fail."

The "because" creates belief.

### Mechanism language patterns

**The "Unlike" Pattern:**

> "Unlike traditional approaches that [common method], we use [mechanism] to [benefit]."

**The "Discovery" Pattern:**

> "After [research/testing/experience], we discovered that [insight]. That's why we developed [mechanism]."

**The "The Problem With" Pattern:**

> "The problem with [common approach] is [flaw]. [Mechanism] solves this by [how]."

**The "The Secret" Pattern:**

> "The secret most [experts] won't tell you is [insight]. [Mechanism] leverages this by [how]."

### Testing your mechanism

- **Believability Test:** Does the mechanism make the promise MORE believable? If not, it's not working.
- **Differentiation Test:** Could a competitor claim the exact same mechanism? If yes, make it more specific.
- **Explanation Test:** Can you explain why the mechanism works in 2-3 sentences? If not, simplify.
- **"That's different" Test:** Do prospects say "I haven't tried that" or "That makes sense"? If they say "I've heard that before," the mechanism isn't differentiated enough.

### Mechanism mistakes to avoid

1. **No mechanism at all** - Just making promises without explaining how
2. **Generic mechanism** - "We use proven strategies" (says nothing)
3. **Unbelievable mechanism** - Sounds too good or too magical
4. **Unexplained mechanism** - Named but never explained
5. **Irrelevant mechanism** - Technically different but doesn't affect results

### How mechanism fits positioning

The mechanism directly impacts "Perceived Likelihood of Achievement" in any value equation:

**Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time × Effort)**

A strong mechanism increases perceived likelihood because it gives the prospect a REASON to believe results are achievable.

- Without a mechanism: "Maybe this will work for me."
- With a mechanism: "This approach addresses why things haven't worked before. It should work this time."

## Example: SaaS project management tool

### Context gathered:

- **Product:** Project management software
- **Category:** Project management / team collaboration
- **Current positioning:** "Better project management for teams"
- **Competitors:** Asana, Monday, Notion, Linear, ClickUp
- **Audience:** Small tech teams (5-15 people)
- **Differentiators:** Extremely simple, no learning curve, opinionated workflows, built by developers who hated enterprise PM tools

### Angles identified:

---

## Angle 1: The "Anti-Complexity" Angle

**Positioning:** The project management tool that refuses to add features.

**The logic:** Market leaders have become bloated. Teams spend more time configuring tools than using them. This positions as the antidote - deliberate simplicity.

**Who it's for:** Teams frustrated by complex tools, feature fatigue, constant reconfiguration.

**What it's against:** Asana, Monday, ClickUp - the feature-laden options that require training and ongoing admin.

**Headline example:** "All the project management you need. Nothing you don't."

**Risk/consideration:** Won't appeal to enterprises or teams that genuinely need advanced features. Must commit to staying simple.

---

## Angle 2: The "Built for Builders" Angle

**Positioning:** Project management for teams that ship, not teams that plan.

**The logic:** Most PM tools optimize for reporting, oversight, and planning. This one optimizes for execution speed. Developer-first philosophy.

**Who it's for:** Shipping-focused tech teams who see PM tools as overhead to minimize.

**What it's against:** Tools that feel like surveillance or bureaucracy. "Manager tools."

**Headline example:** "Less planning. More shipping."

**Risk/consideration:** Might alienate more process-heavy teams or those with compliance needs.

---

## Angle 3: The "5-Minute Onboarding" Angle

**Positioning:** The only PM tool your team will actually use without training.

**The logic:** Tool adoption fails because of learning curve. This removes the learning curve entirely. Specific, measurable claim.

**Who it's for:** Team leads tired of fighting tool adoption. Teams that have tried and abandoned other tools.

**What it's against:** Complex tools that require champions, training, and constant nudging.

**Headline example:** "From signup to productive team: 5 minutes."

**Risk/consideration:** Must actually deliver on the 5-minute promise. Sets concrete expectation.

---

## Angle 4: The "Opinionated Workflow" Angle

**Positioning:** The PM tool with opinions so you don't need to have debates.

**The logic:** Flexibility sounds good but causes paralysis. Teams waste hours deciding "how to use" their tools. This tool makes the decisions for you.

**Who it's for:** Teams that don't want to build a process from scratch. Teams that value convention over configuration.

**What it's against:** "Infinitely flexible" tools like Notion that require building your own system.

**Headline example:** "We already built the process. You just use it."

**Risk/consideration:** Will actively turn off teams that want customization. That's the point.

---

## Angle 5: The "Anti-Enterprise" Angle

**Positioning:** Project management for teams too small to need enterprise tools but too serious for sticky notes.

**The logic:** Clear segment definition. Neither individual productivity nor enterprise complexity. The middle ground.

**Who it's for:** 5-15 person teams who've outgrown basic tools but don't need enterprise features.

**What it's against:** Both enterprise PM tools (overkill) and too-simple tools (not enough).

**Headline example:** "Powerful enough for real teams. Simple enough to actually use."

**Risk/consideration:** Limits TAM intentionally. Can't expand to enterprise without abandoning positioning.

---

## Recommended angle

**Lead with Angle 1 (Anti-Complexity)** because:

- Clearest contrast with market leaders
- Immediately differentiating
- Resonates emotionally (everyone's frustrated with bloated tools)
- Defensible through product decisions

**Support with Angle 3 (5-Minute Onboarding)** as proof point - concrete evidence of the anti-complexity philosophy.

---

## How to choose between angles

When you have multiple strong angles:

1. **Which is most defensible long-term?** (Competitors can copy features, harder to copy philosophy)
2. **Which resonates with highest-value customers?** (Not just most customers)
3. **Which can you actually deliver on?** (Don't claim what you can't prove)
4. **Which is most differentiated in current market?** (Novelty matters)
5. **Which feels most authentic to who you are?** (Sustainable positioning)

## Common angle types

### 1. The Segment Specialist

"[Product] for [specific audience]"

- Targets underserved niche
- Implicit: "We understand you better than generalist tools"
- Example: "Linear - built for software teams"

### 2. The Contrarian

"Unlike [common approach], we [different approach]"

- Challenges category orthodoxy
- Implicit: "Everyone else is wrong"
- Example: "Basecamp - the anti-project-management tool"

### 3. The Process Innovator

"[Product] with [unique methodology]"

- New way of solving old problem
- Implicit: "New approach = better results"
- Example: "Loom - async video for work"

### 4. The Speed Claim

"[Outcome] in [faster timeframe]"

- Specific, measurable improvement
- Implicit: "We remove friction"
- Example: "Stripe - live in minutes"

### 5. The Value Unbundler

"[High-value capability] without [previous requirement]"

- Makes expensive/complex accessible
- Implicit: "What used to require X, now anyone can do"
- Example: "Webflow - professional sites without code"

### 6. The Results Focus

"[Specific outcome] guaranteed"

- Promises result, not just tool
- Implicit: "We care about your success, not just selling software"
- Example: "QuickBooks - get paid 2x faster"

### 7. The Anti-Feature

"[Category] without the [hated thing]"

- Removes widely-hated element
- Implicit: "We heard your complaints"
- Example: "Calendly - scheduling without the back-and-forth"

## Positioning vs messaging

**Positioning** = Strategic decision about where you sit in market
**Messaging** = How you communicate that position

This skill outputs positioning angles. Those angles then inform:

- Headlines (direct-response-copy skill)
- Content topics (seo-content skill)
- Lead magnets (lead-magnet skill)
- Email sequences (email-sequences skill)

The positioning angle should be consistent across all touchpoints.

## Red flags in positioning

Watch out for:

1. **"We're better at everything"** - Not credible. Pick battles.
2. **"We're the complete solution"** - Usually means generic.
3. **"We're for everyone"** - Means you're for no one.
4. **"We have more features"** - Features aren't positioning.
5. **"We're cheaper"** - Price is weak positioning (unless intentional strategy).
6. **Can't name the alternative** - If you can't say what you're against, position is too vague.

---

## The test

Good positioning passes these tests:

1. **Is it specific?** "Project management for everyone" = fail. "Project management for shipping teams under 15 people" = pass.

2. **Is it different?** If competitors could say the same thing, it's not differentiated.

3. **Is it believable?** Can you prove this claim? Is there evidence?

4. **Is it relevant?** Does the target audience actually care about this difference?

5. **Can you state the opposite?** Good positioning implies what you're NOT. If the opposite doesn't exist, position is too generic.

Good: "Simple over powerful" (opposite: "Powerful over simple")
Bad: "High quality" (opposite "Low quality" - no one claims that)
