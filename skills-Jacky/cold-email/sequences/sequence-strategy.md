## Why Sequences Work

### The Follow-Up Gap

**Reality Check**:
- 44% of salespeople give up after one follow-up
- 80% of sales require 5+ touches
- The gap between persistence and success is enormous

### Why People Don't Reply to Email 1

Even great first emails often get no response because:

**Timing**: They saw it at the wrong moment
**Inbox Burial**: It got lost in the flood
**Distraction**: They meant to reply but forgot
**Uncertainty**: They need more information to decide
**Priority**: Other things took precedence
**Skepticism**: They need more trust before engaging

Follow-ups address all of these.

### The Mere Exposure Effect

Psychology shows that familiarity breeds liking. Each email:
- Increases recognition of your name
- Builds brand awareness
- Demonstrates persistence (a positive trait)
- Provides another opportunity to hit the right moment

### Persistence vs. Annoyance

There's a line between persistent and annoying:

**Persistent** (Good):
- Provides new value each time
- Respects their time
- Uses appropriate spacing
- Offers easy opt-out
- Stops after reasonable attempts

**Annoying** (Bad):
- Same message repeated
- Too frequent
- Guilt-inducing language
- No new value
- Ignores signals to stop

## Optimal Number of Emails

### The Research

Studies on cold email sequences show:

| Sequence Length | Reply Rate Pattern |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 email | Baseline |
| 2-3 emails | +30-50% vs baseline |
| 4-5 emails | +60-80% vs baseline |
| 6-7 emails | +80-100% vs baseline |
| 8+ emails | Diminishing returns |

### Recommended Sequence Lengths

**Standard Sequence**: 5-7 emails
- Balances persistence with respect
- Covers multiple angles
- Gives adequate time to respond
- Most common in successful campaigns

**Short Sequence**: 3-4 emails
- For time-sensitive campaigns
- For highly targeted, low-volume outreach
- When testing new messaging

**Extended Sequence**: 8-10 emails
- For high-value prospects
- When you have many angles to try
- For complex products with long sales cycles

### Where Most Replies Come

**Distribution of replies by email number**:
- Email 1: 30-40% of total replies
- Email 2: 20-25%
- Email 3: 15-20%
- Email 4: 10-15%
- Email 5+: 5-10% each

Key insight: A significant portion (20-30%+) of replies come from email 3 onwards. Stopping after email 2 leaves substantial opportunity on the table.

## Timing Between Emails

### Recommended Timing

| Transition | Days to Wait | Rationale |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| Email 1 → 2 | 3-4 days | Quick follow-up while top of mind |
| Email 2 → 3 | 4-5 days | Slightly longer, different angle |
| Email 3 → 4 | 5-7 days | Giving more space |
| Email 4 → 5 | 7-10 days | Longer gap, breakup coming |
| Email 5 → 6 | 7-14 days | Extended pause |
| Email 6+ | 14+ days | Final attempts |

### Factors Affecting Timing

**Shorter Gaps (2-3 days)** work better when:
- Time-sensitive offer
- Strong initial engagement (open, click)
- Highly relevant trigger event
- Prospect showed previous interest

**Longer Gaps (7-14 days)** work better when:
- Complex, considered purchase
- Prospect is very senior
- No engagement on previous emails
- Enterprise sales cycles

### Day of Week Considerations

**Best Days**: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
**Moderate**: Monday (morning inbox clearing), Friday (wind-down mode)
**Avoid**: Saturday, Sunday (unless targeting founders who work weekends)

### Time of Day Considerations

**Best Times**:
- 8-10 AM (morning email checking)
- 2-4 PM (afternoon lull)

**Avoid**:
- Before 7 AM (might get buried)
- After 6 PM (end of day)
- During common meeting times (11 AM-1 PM)

## Multi-Channel Sequences

### The Case for Multi-Channel

Email alone reaches ~30-40% of prospects effectively. Adding channels increases touch points and improves response rates.

### Common Multi-Channel Combinations

**Email + LinkedIn**:
- Most common B2B combination
- LinkedIn for warming, email for details
- LinkedIn for follow-up visibility

**Email + Phone**:
- Phone for high-value prospects
- Call after email engagement
- Voicemail + email one-two punch

**Email + Video**:
- Video for personalization
- Loom/Vidyard for custom messages
- Stands out in inbox

### Multi-Channel Sequence Example

**Day 1**: Email 1 (Initial outreach)
**Day 3**: LinkedIn connection request with note
**Day 5**: Email 2 (Follow-up, reference LinkedIn)
**Day 7**: LinkedIn message (if connected)
**Day 10**: Email 3 (New angle)
**Day 14**: Email 4 (Value add)
**Day 18**: LinkedIn engagement (comment on their post)
**Day 21**: Email 5 (Breakup)

### Multi-Channel Best Practices

**Do**:
- Coordinate messaging across channels
- Reference previous touches naturally
- Use each channel for its strengths
- Space out multi-channel touches

**Don't**:
- Hit them on all channels the same day
- Duplicate exact messaging across channels
- Ignore channel-specific etiquette
- Over-automate and lose personalization

## When to Stop

### Clear Stop Signals

**Stop immediately when**:
- They explicitly ask you to stop
- They unsubscribe
- They mark as spam
- They say "no" clearly

**Stop soon when**:
- Multiple bounces (email invalid)
- They've left the company
- Clear disinterest (harsh negative response)

### The Breakup Email

The final email in a sequence should be a "breakup":

**Purpose**:
- Give them one last chance
- Clear your pipeline
- Sometimes triggers response

**Tone**:
- Not guilt-inducing
- Professional and helpful
- Leaves door open

**Example**:
```
[First name],

I've reached out a few times without hearing back—no worries, I know you're busy.

I'll assume [topic] isn't a priority right now and won't keep emailing.

If things change, I'm here. Just reply whenever.

[Your name]
```

### Post-Sequence Strategy

After a sequence ends without response:

**Option 1: Full Stop**
Remove from outreach, move to long-term nurture

**Option 2: Re-Engagement Sequence**
Wait 3-6 months, restart with fresh angle

**Option 3: Trigger-Based Re-Entry**
Set up alerts for trigger events, restart when one fires

## Sequence Architecture

### The Value Progression Model

Each email should add value, not just follow up:

**Email 1**: Introduction + Primary Value Proposition
**Email 2**: Different Angle + Social Proof
**Email 3**: Resource/Insight (Give Value)
**Email 4**: Case Study/Specific Result
**Email 5**: New Hook or Trigger Reference
**Email 6**: Breakup/Last Chance

### The Angle Rotation Model

Different hooks across the sequence:

**Email 1**: Pain point focus
**Email 2**: Result/outcome focus
**Email 3**: Competitor/industry focus
**Email 4**: Resource/help focus
**Email 5**: Timing/urgency focus
**Email 6**: Honest breakup

### The Escalation Model

Gradually increase commitment ask:

**Email 1**: "Would this be relevant?"
**Email 2**: "Worth a quick chat?"
**Email 3**: "Can I share a resource?"
**Email 4**: "15 minutes this week?"
**Email 5**: "Who should I talk to?"
**Email 6**: "Should I stop emailing?"

## Sequence Types

### Standard Prospecting Sequence

For cold outreach to ICP matches without specific triggers.

- Length: 5-7 emails
- Timing: 3-7 day gaps
- Focus: Value proposition + social proof

### Trigger-Based Sequence

For outreach based on specific events (funding, hiring, etc.).

- Length: 4-5 emails
- Timing: Faster cadence (2-4 day gaps)
- Focus: Relevance to the trigger

### Re-Engagement Sequence

For prospects who went cold or didn't respond to previous sequence.

- Length: 3-4 emails
- Timing: Longer gaps (7-14 days)
- Focus: New angle, fresh value

### Inbound Follow-Up Sequence

For leads who've shown interest but gone quiet.

- Length: 4-6 emails
- Timing: Shorter gaps (2-3 days initially)
- Focus: What they were interested in

### Enterprise Sequence

For complex, high-value deals.

- Length: 8-10 emails
- Timing: Longer gaps (7-14 days)
- Focus: Multi-threaded, multiple stakeholders
- Multi-channel: Email + LinkedIn + Phone

## A/B Testing Sequences

### What to Test

**Sequence-Level Tests**:
- Sequence length (5 vs 7 emails)
- Overall timing (tight vs loose)
- Multi-channel vs email only

**Email-Level Tests**:
- Subject lines
- Opening lines
- CTAs
- Value propositions
- Social proof examples

### Testing Framework

1. **Isolate variables**: Test one thing at a time
2. **Sufficient sample**: 100+ prospects per variant
3. **Clear metrics**: Reply rate as primary, meeting rate as secondary
4. **Statistical significance**: Don't conclude too early
5. **Document learnings**: Build institutional knowledge

### Iteration Cycle

1. Run sequence for 2-4 weeks
2. Analyze results by email
3. Identify underperforming emails
4. Hypothesize improvements
5. Test improved versions
6. Roll out winners
7. Repeat

## Summary: Sequence Strategy Principles

**Follow Up**: Most replies come from follow-ups, not first emails.

**Add Value**: Each email should provide new value, not just poke.

**Respect Boundaries**: Know when to stop and make stopping graceful.

**Multiple Channels**: Email alone reaches a fraction of your potential.

**Test and Iterate**: Sequences should improve over time based on data.

**Architecture Matters**: Plan the full sequence, not just the first email.
