## The Bump Email

### What It Is

The bump email is a brief follow-up that brings your previous email back to the top of their inbox. It's typically Email 2 in a sequence.

### When to Use

- 3-4 days after initial email
- When the first email was strong but got no response
- When you want to stay brief

### Bump Email Framework

```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

[First name],

Quick bump on this—wanted to make sure it didn't get buried.

[Brief restatement of value or CTA]

[Your name]
```

### Bump Email Examples

**Ultra-Short**:
```
Subject: Re: Question about [Company]'s hiring

Bumping this up—still think [brief value] could be relevant for [Company].

Worth a quick chat?
```

**With New Context**:
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

Following up on my note last week.

Since then, I noticed [new observation about them or their industry].

Still think there could be something here. Open to a quick call?
```

**Direct**:
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

[First name],

Did you get a chance to see my email from Tuesday?

[One-sentence value recap]

Let me know if 15 minutes would work to explore.
```

### Bump Email Best Practices

**Do**:
- Keep it very short (under 50 words)
- Reference the previous email
- Include a clear CTA
- Add small new element if possible

**Don't**:
- Apologize for following up
- Repeat the entire first email
- Use guilt-inducing language
- Send too soon (wait 3+ days)

## New Angle Follow-Ups

### What It Is

New angle follow-ups present your value proposition from a different perspective. If Email 1 focused on one benefit, Email 3 highlights another.

### Why It Works

Different people respond to different messages. A prospect who didn't care about "saving time" might care about "beating competitors." New angles find the hook that resonates.

### Common Angles to Rotate

1. **Pain Point**: Focus on the problem they face
2. **Outcome**: Focus on the results they could achieve
3. **Competitor**: Reference what peers/competitors are doing
4. **Risk**: Focus on what they're missing out on
5. **Ease**: Focus on how simple it is
6. **Authority**: Focus on credibility and expertise
7. **Timing**: Focus on why now matters

### New Angle Framework

```
Subject: Different angle

[First name],

Trying a different approach here.

[New angle on your value proposition]

[Social proof relevant to this angle]

[CTA]

[Your name]
```

### New Angle Examples

**From Pain to Outcome**:
```
Email 1: "Most sales teams struggle with [problem]..."

Email 3: "What would it mean for [Company] to hit [specific outcome]?

That's what [Similar Company] achieved in [timeframe] using our approach.

Worth seeing how?"
```

**From Features to Competitors**:
```
Email 1: "We have [features that help with X]..."

Email 4: "Noticed [Competitor] and [Another Competitor] both use [your solution].

There's a reason—[brief explanation].

Curious if [Company] should be looking at this too."
```

**From General to Specific**:
```
Email 1: "We help companies improve their sales process..."

Email 3: "Specifically, we help [role] at [company type] [achieve specific thing].

[Similar Company]'s [title] told us: '[specific quote].'

Relevant to what you're working on?"
```

## Value-Add Follow-Ups

### What It Is

Value-add follow-ups provide something useful regardless of whether they respond—a resource, insight, or tool. This builds goodwill and demonstrates expertise.

### Why It Works

- Shows you're helpful, not just selling
- Provides tangible proof of expertise
- Creates reciprocity
- Differentiates from typical follow-ups

### Value-Add Framework

```
Subject: [Resource] for [Company]/[topic]

[First name],

Following up with something useful regardless of whether we connect.

[Brief description of value you're providing]

[Link or attachment reference]

No strings attached—just thought it might help.

[Soft CTA or none]

[Your name]
```

### Value-Add Examples

**Resource Share**:
```
Subject: Template for your team

[First name],

Regardless of whether we chat, wanted to share a [template/guide/tool] that might help.

It's [brief description]—most [roles] I share it with find it useful.

[Link]

Let me know if you have questions.
```

**Insight Share**:
```
Subject: Something interesting about [industry]

[First name],

Came across something that made me think of [Company].

[Brief insight or data point relevant to them]

Thought you'd want to know. Let me know if you want more context.
```

**Competitive Intelligence**:
```
Subject: What [competitor] just did

[First name],

Noticed [competitor] just [did something relevant].

Here's what it might mean for [Company]: [brief analysis]

Thought you'd want the heads up.
```

### Value-Add Best Practices

**Do**:
- Provide genuinely useful content
- Make it relevant to their situation
- Offer without expectation
- Follow up softly after

**Don't**:
- Gate content behind a meeting
- Provide fluff disguised as value
- Attach large files without warning
- Make it all about your product

## Social Proof Follow-Ups

### What It Is

Social proof follow-ups highlight customer results, testimonials, or industry adoption to build credibility.

### Why It Works

- Addresses "why should I believe you?" objection
- Demonstrates real-world results
- Shows you understand their world
- Reduces perceived risk

### Social Proof Framework

```
Subject: How [Similar Company] achieved [result]

[First name],

Thought you might find this relevant.

[Brief case study: Company + Challenge + Result]

Their [title] said: "[Brief quote]"

Would it help to see how they did it?

[Your name]
```

### Social Proof Examples

**Case Study Focus**:
```
Subject: [Similar Company]'s results

[First name],

Since I hadn't heard back, wanted to share a recent win.

[Similar Company] was in your situation—[brief description]. After [timeframe] with us, they hit [specific result].

Their [title] called it "[brief quote]."

Worth a quick call to see if you could get similar results?
```

**Industry Adoption**:
```
Subject: What [industry] companies are doing

[First name],

Quick update: [X] more [industry] companies signed up this month.

The common theme: they all wanted to [achieve outcome] faster.

Companies like [Names] are already seeing results.

Curious if [Company] should be looking at this too.
```

**Third-Party Validation**:
```
Subject: Why [Publication] featured us

[First name],

Quick share: [Publication] just wrote about our approach to [topic].

[Link or brief excerpt]

Validates what I mentioned in my first email. Worth discussing?
```

## Breakup Emails

### What It Is

The breakup email is the final email in a sequence—giving one last chance while gracefully closing the loop.

### Why It Works

- Creates scarcity/urgency
- Provides clean ending
- Often triggers responses from fence-sitters
- Shows professional persistence, then respect

### Breakup Email Framework

```
Subject: Closing the loop / Should I close your file?

[First name],

I've reached out a few times without hearing back—I get it, you're busy.

I'll assume [topic] isn't a priority right now and won't keep emailing.

If things change, I'm here. Just reply whenever.

[Your name]
```

### Breakup Email Variations

**The Helpful Goodbye**:
```
Subject: One last thing before I go

[First name],

This is my last note—wanted to leave you with something useful regardless.

[Resource/insight/offer]

If [topic] becomes a priority down the road, you know where to find me.

Best of luck with everything.
```

**The Direct Question**:
```
Subject: Should I stop emailing?

[First name],

Been trying to connect about [topic] without luck.

Would rather not keep filling your inbox if this isn't relevant.

Should I stop reaching out, or is there a better time to reconnect?
```

**The Honest Breakup**:
```
Subject: Closing the loop

[First name],

I've sent several emails without a response. No hard feelings—I know how it is.

I'm going to pause outreach for now. If [challenge] becomes a priority later, feel free to reach back.

Wishing you and [Company] continued success.
```

### Breakup Email Best Practices

**Do**:
- Keep it brief and professional
- Leave the door open
- Remove guilt or pressure
- Accept the outcome gracefully

**Don't**:
- Make them feel bad for not responding
- Threaten or use ultimatums
- Burn bridges
- Actually close forever (they might respond later)

## Re-Engagement Sequences

### What It Is

Re-engagement sequences reach out to prospects who previously didn't respond, but after enough time has passed or circumstances have changed.

### When to Re-Engage

- 3-6 months after sequence ended
- When trigger event occurs (funding, hiring, role change)
- When you have genuinely new information
- When their situation likely changed

### Re-Engagement Framework

```
Subject: Things have changed / Reaching back

[First name],

It's been a while since we connected. Wanted to reach back because:

[Reason for re-engagement: new product, new case study, their trigger event]

Thought this might be relevant to [Company] now.

Worth reconnecting?

[Your name]
```

### Re-Engagement Examples

**New Development**:
```
Subject: Quick update since we last talked

[First name],

We connected [timeframe] ago—timing wasn't right then.

Since then, we've [new development: new feature, new case study, new integration].

Thought of [Company] because [specific reason].

Worth revisiting?
```

**Their Trigger**:
```
Subject: Congrats on [their news]

[First name],

Saw [their news/trigger event]. Congrats!

We connected [timeframe] ago about [topic]. Given [trigger event], this might be more relevant now.

Would a quick catch-up make sense?
```

**Time-Based**:
```
Subject: Checking back in

[First name],

It's been [timeframe] since we last connected. Wanted to see where things stand.

Is [challenge/topic] something [Company] is focused on now?

If so, [brief value reminder]. If not, let me know and I'll check back later.
```

## Follow-Up by Engagement Level

### No Engagement (No Opens)

If they're not opening, the subject line isn't working.

**Strategy**:
- Try different subject line approaches
- Check if email is valid
- Vary send times
- Consider they're not at that address

### Opened But No Reply

They're interested enough to open, but not to reply.

**Strategy**:
- Body copy or CTA isn't resonating
- Try new angles on value proposition
- Shorter emails
- Different CTAs

### Clicked But No Reply

High interest signal—they engaged with content.

**Strategy**:
- More direct follow-up
- Reference what they clicked
- Faster follow-up timing
- Consider phone call

### Replied But Went Cold

They were interested, then disappeared.

**Strategy**:
- Reference their last message
- Ask if circumstances changed
- Provide easy re-entry point
- Respect their time

## Summary: Follow-Up Principles

**Every Email Needs a Purpose**: "Just following up" isn't a strategy.

**Add New Value Each Time**: Different angle, new resource, fresh social proof.

**Respect Evolves**: Earlier emails can be more frequent; later emails give more space.

**End Gracefully**: Breakup emails should leave doors open, not burn bridges.

**Re-Engagement Is Allowed**: People who didn't respond once might respond later.

**Match Follow-Up to Engagement**: Adjust strategy based on how they're interacting (or not).
