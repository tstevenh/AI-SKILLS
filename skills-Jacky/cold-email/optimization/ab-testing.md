# A/B Testing

A/B testing is how you turn cold email from guessing into science. This chapter covers what to test, how to test it, and how to interpret results.

## Why A/B Testing Matters

**Without Testing**: You rely on intuition. Results are random.
**With Testing**: You know what works. Results improve systematically.

## What to Test

### High-Impact Tests (Start Here)

**Subject Lines** (Biggest impact on opens)
- Question vs. Statement
- Personalized vs. Generic
- Short vs. Medium length
- Curiosity vs. Direct

**Opening Lines** (Impact on read-through)
- Observation vs. Compliment
- Specific vs. General
- Long vs. Short

**CTAs** (Impact on replies)
- Question vs. Statement
- Interest-based vs. Commitment
- With vs. Without calendar link

### Medium-Impact Tests

**Value Proposition Angle**
- Pain focus vs. Outcome focus
- Feature vs. Benefit
- General vs. Specific

**Social Proof**
- With vs. Without
- Company name vs. Category reference
- Result vs. Quote

**Email Length**
- Short (50 words) vs. Medium (100 words)
- With vs. Without PS line

### Lower-Impact Tests

**Formatting**
- Signature style
- Line breaks
- Bullet points vs. Prose

**Timing**
- Day of week
- Time of day
- Sequence spacing

## Testing Framework

### 1. Hypothesis
State what you expect to happen.
*"Subject lines with questions will have higher open rates than statements."*

### 2. Variables
- **Control (A)**: Current approach
- **Variation (B)**: New approach
- **Isolation**: Change only ONE element

### 3. Sample Size
**Minimum**: 100 recipients per variant
**Better**: 250+ per variant
**Best**: 500+ per variant

### 4. Duration
Run until you have:
- Sufficient sample size
- Statistical significance (typically 95%)
- Enough time for sequences to complete

### 5. Metrics
- Primary: Reply rate (most reliable)
- Secondary: Open rate, Positive reply rate, Meeting rate

### 6. Analysis
- Did variation beat control?
- Is the difference statistically significant?
- What's the practical significance?

---

## Statistical Significance

### Why It Matters
Small sample sizes can show differences that are just random chance. Statistical significance tells you if the difference is real.

### Quick Significance Check

| Sample Size (per variant) | Difference Needed |
|---------------------------|-------------------|
| 100 | ~5% absolute |
| 250 | ~3% absolute |
| 500 | ~2% absolute |
| 1,000 | ~1.5% absolute |

*Example: With 100 emails per variant, you need ~5% difference to be confident (50% vs 45%).*

### Tools for Calculation
- AB Test Calculator (various online tools)
- Google Analytics experiments
- Most cold email tools have built-in stats

## Test Examples

### Subject Line Test
**Control A**: "Quick question about your sales process"
**Variation B**: "Noticed something about Acme's approach"

**Hypothesis**: Observation-based subject lines outperform question-based
**Metric**: Open rate
**Sample**: 200 per variant
**Duration**: 1 week

### CTA Test
**Control A**: "Would a quick chat be worth it?"
**Variation B**: "Interested in seeing how it works?"

**Hypothesis**: Lower-commitment CTAs get more replies
**Metric**: Reply rate
**Sample**: 150 per variant
**Duration**: Full sequence completion

### Opening Line Test
**Control A**: Generic compliment about company
**Variation B**: Specific reference to LinkedIn post

**Hypothesis**: Specific personalization improves read-through and reply
**Metric**: Reply rate
**Sample**: 200 per variant
**Duration**: Full sequence

## Testing Best Practices

### Do
- Test one variable at a time
- Use large enough sample sizes
- Wait for statistical significance
- Document all tests and results
- Apply learnings consistently

### Don't
- Change multiple things at once
- Conclude too early
- Ignore practical significance
- Test trivial differences
- Forget about the testing

### Test Prioritization

**Test First**: Elements with biggest potential impact
1. Subject lines (affects all downstream metrics)
2. CTAs (directly affects replies)
3. Opening lines (affects read-through)
4. Value proposition (affects resonance)

**Test Later**: Lower-impact or harder-to-measure elements
5. Sequence length
6. Timing
7. Formatting

## Iteration Cycle

### The Testing Loop
```
1. Identify biggest opportunity (what's underperforming?)
     ↓
2. Form hypothesis (what might improve it?)
     ↓
3. Design test (A/B with one variable)
     ↓
4. Run test (sufficient sample and duration)
     ↓
5. Analyze results (statistical + practical significance)
     ↓
6. Implement winner (roll out broadly)
     ↓
7. Document learning (build institutional knowledge)
     ↓
8. Repeat (next biggest opportunity)
```

### Continuous Improvement
- Run 2-4 tests per month
- Always have a test running
- Build on previous learnings
- Keep a testing log

---

## Common Testing Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Testing Too Many Things**
Change one thing at a time, or you won't know what caused the difference.

**Mistake 2: Small Sample Sizes**
100 emails isn't enough to draw conclusions. Aim for 200-500 minimum.

**Mistake 3: Stopping Early**
"Variant B is winning after 50 emails!" Wait for significance.

**Mistake 4: Ignoring Context**
Results may vary by segment. What works for SMB might not work for Enterprise.

**Mistake 5: Not Documenting**
If you don't write it down, you'll forget what you learned.

---

## Summary: Testing Principles

**Be Scientific**: One variable, sufficient sample, statistical significance.

**Prioritize Impact**: Test high-impact elements first.

**Iterate Continuously**: Testing is ongoing, not one-time.

**Document Everything**: Build knowledge over time.

**Act on Learnings**: Apply winners; don't just test.
