## The First 7 Words Rule

### The Science Behind 7 Words

Eye-tracking and engagement studies reveal that readers make a decision about whether to continue reading within the first 7 words of any text. In cold email, this is critical because:

- **Inbox preview**: Many clients show 7-15 words of body text
- **Mobile behavior**: First line often visible without opening
- **Attention span**: Readers scan, don't read
- **Spam detection**: Recipients quickly categorize emails

### What First 7 Words Must Accomplish

1. **Signal relevance**: "This is for me"
2. **Demonstrate research**: "They know who I am"
3. **Create intrigue**: "I want to know more"
4. **Avoid spam signals**: "This isn't a mass email"

### The Preview Text Opportunity

The first line often shows in the inbox preview alongside the subject line:

**Example Inbox View**:
```
From: John Smith
Subject: Question about Acme's hiring
Preview: Noticed you're building out the sales team...
```

This preview is additional real estate to earn the open/read. Don't waste it on "Hi [Name], I hope this email finds you well."

## Why Opening Lines Matter

### The Funnel Drop-Off

Consider this email funnel:
- 100 emails sent
- 55 opened (subject line worked)
- **30 read past first line** (opening line filter)
- 15 read to CTA
- 5 replies

The opening line is responsible for a significant drop-off. Improving it directly improves replies.

### What Recipients Are Thinking

When they open and see your first line, they're asking:
- "Who is this person?"
- "Why are they emailing me?"
- "Is this relevant to me?"
- "Is this worth my time?"

Your opening line must answer these questions favorably—instantly.

### Good vs. Bad First Lines

**Bad Opening Lines**:
- "I hope this email finds you well" (Generic, wasted space)
- "My name is John and I work at Acme" (Self-focused)
- "I wanted to reach out to you because" (Obvious padding)
- "I came across your profile and thought" (Generic)
- "I know you're busy, but" (Apologetic, weak)

**Good Opening Lines**:
- "Your LinkedIn post about hiring challenges resonated" (Specific, researched)
- "Congrats on the Series B—exciting milestone" (Timely, relevant)
- "Noticed Acme is expanding to Europe" (Demonstrates research)
- "Fellow YC founder here—Sarah suggested I reach out" (Warm connection)

## Opening Line Frameworks

### Framework 1: The Observation

Reference something specific you observed about them or their company.

**Pattern**: "I noticed [specific observation about them/company]..."

**Examples**:
- "Noticed your team doubled this quarter—impressive growth."
- "Saw your LinkedIn post about the challenges of scaling sales."
- "Your approach to content marketing caught my attention."

**Why It Works**: Demonstrates research and establishes relevance immediately.

### Framework 2: The Compliment

Start with genuine, specific praise about something they've done.

**Pattern**: "[Specific compliment about their work/achievement]..."

**Examples**:
- "Your talk at SaaStr on product-led growth was excellent—especially the bit about activation metrics."
- "The new Acme onboarding flow is really smooth. Nice work."
- "Been following your newsletter—the piece on pricing strategy was spot-on."

**Why It Works**: Flattery works (when genuine). Creates positive first impression.

### Framework 3: The Trigger Event

Reference a recent event as the reason for reaching out.

**Pattern**: "Saw [trigger event]—[connection to why you're emailing]..."

**Examples**:
- "Congrats on the new VP of Sales hire—building out the team is exciting but challenging."
- "Saw Acme's product launch last week—great positioning."
- "Noticed you're hiring 5 SDRs—scaling outbound is probably top of mind."

**Why It Works**: Timeliness signals relevance. Shows you're paying attention.

### Framework 4: The Mutual Connection

Leverage a shared connection, experience, or community.

**Pattern**: "[Mutual connection/experience] [connection point]..."

**Examples**:
- "Sarah from TechCo suggested I reach out—she mentioned you're..."
- "Fellow YC founder here—saw your Demo Day pitch."
- "We met briefly at SaaStock—you mentioned struggling with..."

**Why It Works**: Warm introductions earn attention. Shared experiences create rapport.

### Framework 5: The Challenge Acknowledgment

Reference a common challenge they likely face.

**Pattern**: "Most [role/company type] I talk to are dealing with [challenge]..."

**Examples**:
- "Most sales leaders I talk to are drowning in manual data entry."
- "Scaling from 10 to 50 reps is notoriously difficult—curious how you're approaching it."
- "Series A companies often struggle with the shift from founder-led sales."

**Why It Works**: Shows you understand their world. Creates "yes, that's me" response.

### Framework 6: The Direct Approach

Skip pleasantries and state your purpose immediately.

**Pattern**: "[Direct statement of value/purpose]..."

**Examples**:
- "I help companies like Acme reduce their sales cycle by 30%."
- "Quick question: is outbound a priority for Acme this quarter?"
- "Reaching out because we've helped 3 of your competitors solve [problem]."

**Why It Works**: Respects their time. Clear and confident.

## Personalization Techniques

### Research Sources for Personalization

**LinkedIn Profile**:
- Recent posts and articles
- Job changes
- Skills endorsements
- Activity and engagement
- Education and background

**Company Sources**:
- Press releases and news
- Blog and content
- Product updates
- Job postings
- Social media

**Third-Party Sources**:
- Podcast appearances
- Conference talks
- Industry publications
- Review sites

### Personalization Depth Levels

**Level 1: Basic** (Low effort, low impact)
- First name
- Company name
- Job title

**Level 2: Surface** (Medium effort, medium impact)
- Industry reference
- Company size reference
- Geographic reference

**Level 3: Researched** (High effort, high impact)
- Recent LinkedIn post
- Company announcement
- Specific product mention
- Content they created

**Level 4: Deep** (Very high effort, very high impact)
- Connection to mutual contact
- Reference to specific challenge they've mentioned
- Insight based on multiple data points

### Personalization Time Investment

**ROI of Personalization**:
- Level 1: 30 seconds → 5% reply rate lift
- Level 2: 2 minutes → 15% reply rate lift
- Level 3: 5 minutes → 40% reply rate lift
- Level 4: 15+ minutes → 75%+ reply rate lift

For high-value prospects, deep personalization is worth it.

### Scaling Personalization

**For Volume**: Use tools like Clay to pull personalization data automatically (LinkedIn posts, company news, etc.)

**For Quality**: Spend time on highest-value prospects manually.

**Hybrid**: Automate research, personalize writing.

## Pattern Interrupts

### What Is a Pattern Interrupt

A pattern interrupt is an opening that breaks expected patterns and forces attention.

**Why Pattern Interrupts Work**:
- Inboxes are full of predictable emails
- The brain notices novelty
- Unexpected = memorable

### Pattern Interrupt Examples

**Self-Deprecating**:
"I'm about to send a cold email—but bear with me."

**Unexpected Honesty**:
"You probably get 20 emails like this a day. Here's why this one's different."

**Contrarian Statement**:
"Unpopular opinion: most [industry] advice is wrong. Here's why."

**Vulnerability**:
"I wasn't going to send this, but..."

**Humor** (use carefully):
"My marketing team would hate this email. But I think you'll appreciate the directness."

### Pattern Interrupt Cautions

- **Can backfire**: Might come across as gimmicky
- **Hard to scale**: Genuine pattern interrupts are hard to template
- **Audience-dependent**: Some audiences appreciate it; others don't
- **Use sparingly**: If every email is a pattern interrupt, none are

## Compliment Openers

### The Art of the Genuine Compliment

**Rules for Compliment Openers**:
1. Be specific (not "great company")
2. Be genuine (don't force it)
3. Be brief (don't over-flatter)
4. Be relevant (connect to why you're emailing)

### Good vs. Bad Compliments

**Bad** (Generic, obviously templated):
- "I love what you're doing at Acme!"
- "Your company is so impressive!"
- "You have a great LinkedIn profile!"

**Good** (Specific, genuine, brief):
- "Your recent post on pricing strategy was the clearest explanation I've seen."
- "The new product landing page is really well done—the value prop is instantly clear."
- "Your talk at SaaStr about sales compensation was excellent—especially the point about SPIFs."

### Compliment-to-Pitch Bridge

The compliment should naturally lead to your reason for emailing:

**Compliment**: "Your approach to content marketing is producing great results—Acme's blog traffic growth is impressive."

**Bridge**: "Most companies at your stage start looking at [relevant solution] around now..."

**Pitch**: "We help with exactly that. Would it be worth 15 minutes to see if we could help?"

## Trigger Event Openers

### High-Value Trigger Events

**Funding Events**:
- New round closed
- IPO announcement
- PE acquisition

**People Events**:
- New executive hired
- Role change
- Departure of key person

**Company Events**:
- Product launch
- Geographic expansion
- Partnership announcement
- Acquisition

**Market Events**:
- Industry regulation change
- Competitor news
- Market shift

### Trigger Event Timing

**Optimal Timing**:
- Funding: 2-8 weeks after announcement
- New hire: 2-4 weeks after start
- Product launch: 1-4 weeks after
- News: Within 1 week (fresher = better)

**Too Early**: Seems opportunistic
**Too Late**: Old news, less relevant

### Trigger Event Examples

**Funding**:
"Congrats on the Series B—scaling from here usually means [relevant challenge]. We help with exactly that."

**New Hire**:
"Saw you just joined as VP of Sales—the first 90 days are critical. Most new VPs I talk to are looking at [relevant area]."

**Product Launch**:
"Nice work on the new [product] launch—the positioning is really clear. As you scale marketing for it, [relevant connection]..."

## Mutual Connection Openers

### The Power of Mutual Connections

Mutual connections create instant trust:
- **Credibility**: Someone they trust vouches for you
- **Obligation**: Social reciprocity to at least respond
- **Warmth**: Feels like a warm introduction

### Types of Mutual Connections

**Direct Connection**:
"[Name] suggested I reach out..."

**Shared Experience**:
"Fellow [conference/event] attendee..."

**Shared Community**:
"Fellow [community/group] member..."

**Shared Background**:
"Fellow [alma mater/previous company]..."

**Shared Interest**:
"Noticed we're both interested in [topic]..."

### Using Mutual Connections Ethically

**Do**:
- Ask permission to use someone's name
- Only reference genuine connections
- Explain why they suggested reaching out

**Don't**:
- Name-drop without permission
- Exaggerate the relationship
- Claim connections that don't exist

### Mutual Connection Examples

**With Permission**:
"[Name] mentioned you're looking into [topic]—she thought we should connect because we helped her team with exactly that."

**Shared Experience**:
"We were both at [event] last month—your panel on [topic] was great. Wanted to follow up on something you mentioned..."

**Shared Community**:
"Fellow [Slack community] member here—saw your question about [topic] and thought I could help."

## What to Avoid

### Opening Line Killers

**Generic Pleasantries**:
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "Happy [day of week]!"
- "Hope you had a great weekend"
- "Trust you're doing well"

**Self-Focused Starts**:
- "My name is..."
- "I work at..."
- "We are a company that..."
- "I wanted to reach out because..."

**Apologetic Language**:
- "I know you're busy, but..."
- "Sorry to bother you..."
- "I hate to interrupt, but..."
- "I don't want to take up too much of your time..."

**Obvious Cold Email Signals**:
- "I came across your profile..."
- "I noticed your company on LinkedIn..."
- "I found your information and thought..."

**Fake Familiarity**:
- "How's your day going?"
- "How's everything at [Company]?"
- "Long time!"

### Why These Don't Work

**Generic**: Signals mass email, no personalization
**Self-Focused**: Doesn't answer "what's in it for me?"
**Apologetic**: Undermines your value; starts with weakness
**Obvious**: Screams "cold email, delete me"
**Fake**: Rings false, damages trust immediately

## Opening Line Checklist

### Before Sending

- [ ] First 7 words demonstrate value or relevance
- [ ] Personalization is specific (not just first name)
- [ ] No generic pleasantries ("hope you're well")
- [ ] Not self-focused ("my name is...")
- [ ] Not apologetic ("sorry to bother you")
- [ ] Connects logically to the rest of the email
- [ ] Reads naturally (spoken aloud test)
- [ ] Based on genuine research/observation

### Quality Check Questions

1. Would this opening make ME want to keep reading?
2. Does it show I actually researched this person?
3. Is it specific enough that it couldn't apply to anyone else?
4. Does it connect naturally to my pitch?
5. Would this work as a conference conversation opener?

## Summary: Opening Line Principles

**The First 7 Words**: Make them count—they determine everything.

**Personalization Wins**: Generic openers get generic results (deleted).

**Show Your Work**: Demonstrate research without being creepy.

**Bridge to Value**: The opener should naturally lead to your pitch.

**Be Human**: Write like you talk, not like a marketing robot.

**Respect Time**: Skip pleasantries and get to the point.
