# Go-to-Market Strategy for SaaS

## Introduction

Your go-to-market (GTM) strategy determines how you bring your product to customers and generate revenue. The right GTM strategy aligns your product, pricing, sales motion, and marketing to efficiently acquire and retain customers. This comprehensive guide covers GTM strategy fundamentals, motion selection, and execution frameworks.

---

## 1. Understanding GTM Strategy

### What is Go-to-Market Strategy?

**GTM Strategy** is the plan for how you will:
- Identify target customers
- Position your product
- Price your offering
- Reach customers
- Sell and deliver
- Support and retain

### GTM Alignment

**Everything Must Align:**

| If This... | Then This... |
|------------|--------------|
| Low ACV product | Self-serve sales motion |
| Complex product | Sales-assisted motion |
| Enterprise target | High-touch sales motion |
| Technical buyer | Developer-focused marketing |
| Non-technical buyer | Business value marketing |

**Misalignment Examples:**
- Low ACV + high-touch sales = Unprofitable
- Complex product + pure self-serve = High churn
- Enterprise target + no security features = Can't close

## 2. GTM Motions

### The Three Primary Motions

**1. Product-Led Growth (PLG)**
```
Product → User → Team → Company

Characteristics:
- Self-serve trial/freemium
- In-product conversion
- Usage-driven expansion
- Minimal sales involvement
```

**Best for:**
- Lower ACV products
- Products with clear self-serve value
- Horizontal tools with broad appeal
- Developer/prosumer tools

**Examples:** Slack, Notion, Figma, Calendly

**2. Marketing-Led Growth (MLG)**
```
Content → Lead → MQL → SQL → Sale

Characteristics:
- Content marketing heavy
- Lead generation focus
- Marketing qualified leads to sales
- Inbound-driven
```

**Best for:**
- Mid-market products
- Products requiring education
- Category creation
- Thought leadership positioning

**Examples:** HubSpot, Drift, Gong

**3. Sales-Led Growth (SLG)**
```
Prospecting → Demo → Proposal → Negotiation → Close

Characteristics:
- Outbound prospecting
- High-touch sales process
- Demo-driven conversion
- Account-based approach
```

**Best for:**
- Higher ACV products
- Complex products
- Enterprise targets
- Regulated industries

**Examples:** Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow

### Choosing Your Motion

**Decision Matrix:**

| Factor | PLG | MLG | SLG |
|--------|-----|-----|-----|
| ACV | <$5K | $5K-$50K | >$25K |
| Complexity | Low | Medium | High |
| Decision maker | User | Manager | Executive |
| Sales cycle | Days | Weeks | Months |
| CAC tolerance | Low | Medium | High |

### Hybrid Motions

**Most successful companies use hybrid approaches:**

**PLG + Sales:**
- PLG for land
- Sales for expand/enterprise
- Example: Slack, Zoom

**MLG + PLG:**
- Content for awareness
- PLG for conversion
- Example: HubSpot

**MLG + SLG:**
- Inbound for leads
- Sales for conversion
- Example: Most B2B SaaS

## 3. Target Market Definition

### Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

**ICP Components:**

| Component | Questions to Answer |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Company Size | How many employees? |
| Industry | Which verticals? |
| Geography | Which regions? |
| Revenue | What company revenue? |
| Technology | What tech stack? |
| Stage | Startup, growth, enterprise? |

**Example ICP:**
```
Ideal Customer Profile: [Product]

Company:
- Size: 50-500 employees
- Industry: B2B SaaS, Technology
- Geography: North America, Western Europe
- Stage: Series A-C startups

Buyer:
- Role: VP Marketing, Head of Demand Gen
- Seniority: Director+
- Budget authority: Yes
- Tech savvy: Medium-high

Behaviors:
- Uses HubSpot or Salesforce
- Active in marketing communities
- Attends SaaS conferences
- Has growth targets

Pain Points:
- Can't attribute revenue to marketing
- Spending inefficiently
- Can't prove ROI
```

## 4. Positioning and Messaging

### Positioning Framework

**April Dunford's Positioning Canvas:**

```
1. Competitive Alternatives
   What would customers do if you didn't exist?

2. Unique Attributes
   What do you have that alternatives don't?

3. Value
   What benefit do those attributes provide?

4. Target Customers
   Who cares most about that value?

5. Market Category
   What context makes your value obvious?
```

**Example:**
```
1. Alternatives: Spreadsheets, general CRM
2. Unique: Built for SaaS, automatic metrics
3. Value: Accurate revenue analytics without manual work
4. Target: B2B SaaS finance and operations
5. Category: SaaS Revenue Analytics Platform
```

### Messaging Hierarchy

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ BRAND PROMISE                                          │
│ "The [category] that [key differentiator]"             │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ VALUE PROPOSITION                                       │
│ For [target], [Product] is [category] that [benefit]   │
│ Unlike [alternative], we [differentiator]              │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ PILLAR 1        │ PILLAR 2        │ PILLAR 3          │
│ [Benefit]       │ [Benefit]       │ [Benefit]         │
│ [Feature]       │ [Feature]       │ [Feature]         │
│ [Proof]         │ [Proof]         │ [Proof]           │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Messaging Examples

**Good Messaging:**
```
Calendly: "Scheduling automation for busy professionals"
- Clear: Knows exactly what it does
- Specific: For professionals
- Benefit: Automation (saves time)

Slack: "Where work happens"
- Aspirational: More than chat
- Inclusive: All work types
- Central: The place, not a tool
```

**Bad Messaging:**
```
"The platform for digital transformation"
- Vague: What does it do?
- Buzzword: Transformation?
- No specificity

"Revolutionary enterprise solution"
- No meaning: Revolutionary how?
- Generic: What solution?
- Forgettable
```

## 5. Channel Strategy

### Acquisition Channel Selection

**Channel Evaluation:**

| Channel | CAC | Volume | Time to ROI | Scalability |
|---------|-----|--------|-------------|-------------|
| SEO/Content | Low | High | Long | High |
| Paid Search | Medium | Medium | Short | Medium |
| Paid Social | Medium | High | Short | High |
| Outbound | High | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Partnerships | Low | Low | Long | Medium |
| Community | Low | Medium | Long | Medium |
| Referral | Very Low | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Events | High | Low | Medium | Low |

### Channel Mix by Stage

**Seed/Early:**
- Founder sales
- Community/network
- Content foundation
- Early SEO investment

**Series A:**
- Scale content
- Add paid (testing)
- Build sales team
- Partnerships exploration

**Series B+:**
- Multi-channel paid
- Full sales org
- Channel partnerships
- Brand investment

### Channel Prioritization Framework

**ICE Score:**
```
ICE = (Impact × Confidence × Ease) / 3

Score each channel 1-10:
- Impact: How many customers?
- Confidence: How sure are you?
- Ease: How easy to execute?
```

**Example:**
| Channel | Impact | Confidence | Ease | ICE |
|---------|--------|------------|------|-----|
| SEO | 8 | 6 | 5 | 6.3 |
| Paid Search | 6 | 8 | 7 | 7.0 |
| Outbound | 5 | 7 | 6 | 6.0 |
| Referral | 4 | 5 | 8 | 5.7 |

Priority: Paid Search > SEO > Outbound > Referral

## 6. Sales Strategy

### Sales Team Structure

**By Stage:**

| Stage | Structure |
|-------|-----------|
| Pre-$1M | Founder(s) selling |
| $1M-$3M | 1-3 AEs, founder assists |
| $3M-$10M | AE team, SDR team, manager |
| $10M+ | Full org, specialists |

### Sales Process Design

**Standard B2B SaaS Sales Process:**

```
1. PROSPECTING
   - Identify target accounts
   - Research contacts
   - Outreach (email, call, social)

2. QUALIFICATION
   - Discovery call
   - BANT/MEDDIC qualification
   - Decision: Pursue or disqualify

3. DEMO
   - Custom demonstration
   - Align to their needs
   - Address questions

4. EVALUATION
   - Trial or pilot
   - Technical review
   - Stakeholder buy-in

5. PROPOSAL
   - Pricing proposal
   - Contract terms
   - ROI documentation

6. NEGOTIATION
   - Address concerns
   - Adjust terms
   - Get to yes

7. CLOSE
   - Contract signature
   - Payment
   - Handoff to CS
```

### Sales Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|------------|--------|
| Activity | Calls, emails, demos | Per rep benchmarks |
| Pipeline | Total opportunity value | 3-4x quota |
| Win Rate | Deals won / Deals created | 20-30% |
| Sales Cycle | Days from opp to close | Segment-specific |
| ACV | Average deal value | Segment-specific |
| Quota Attainment | Revenue / Quota | 80%+ |

## Summary: GTM Strategy Framework

### GTM Checklist

**Foundation:**
- [ ] ICP defined
- [ ] Buyer personas created
- [ ] Positioning established
- [ ] Messaging documented

**Motion:**
- [ ] GTM motion selected
- [ ] Sales process designed
- [ ] Channels prioritized
- [ ] Team structure defined

**Execution:**
- [ ] Quarterly plan created
- [ ] Metrics tracked
- [ ] Weekly reviews scheduled
- [ ] Alignment maintained

### Key Principles

1. **Alignment is everything**: Product, pricing, sales, marketing must align
2. **Start focused**: One ICP, one motion, one channel—then expand
3. **Measure what matters**: Track the right metrics
4. **Iterate constantly**: GTM strategy evolves with your business
5. **Listen to customers**: They tell you what works
