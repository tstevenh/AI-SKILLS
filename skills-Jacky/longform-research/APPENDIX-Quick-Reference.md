# Appendix: Research Methodology Quick Reference

## Research Design Decision Matrix

### Choosing Your Research Approach

**When to use Quantitative Methods:**
- Research question asks "how many," "how much," or "to what extent"
- Goal is to test hypotheses or measure relationships between variables
- Large sample sizes are feasible and desirable
- Generalizability to broader populations is important
- Standardized measurement instruments exist or can be developed
- Statistical analysis will strengthen conclusions
- Replicability is a high priority

**When to use Qualitative Methods:**
- Research question asks "how," "why," or "what is the experience of"
- Goal is to explore, describe, or understand phenomena in depth
- Rich, contextual understanding is more valuable than breadth
- The phenomenon is poorly understood or undertheorized
- Participant perspectives and meanings are central
- Flexibility to follow emerging insights is needed
- Small, purposefully selected samples provide adequate data

**When to use Mixed Methods:**
- Research questions require both breadth and depth
- One method alone cannot fully address the research problem
- Triangulation would strengthen confidence in findings
- Qualitative findings need quantitative confirmation or vice versa
- Complex phenomena require multiple forms of evidence
- Stakeholders need different types of evidence

## Essential Quality Checklists

### Before Starting Research
- [ ] Research questions are clear, specific, and answerable
- [ ] Literature review is comprehensive and current
- [ ] Methodology is appropriate for questions
- [ ] Ethical approvals obtained
- [ ] Resources and timeline are realistic
- [ ] Team roles and responsibilities defined
- [ ] Data management plan established
- [ ] Risk assessment completed

### During Data Collection
- [ ] Protocols are being followed consistently
- [ ] Data quality is monitored regularly
- [ ] Deviations from protocol are documented
- [ ] Participant welfare is prioritized
- [ ] Data is securely stored and backed up
- [ ] Progress against timeline is tracked
- [ ] Emerging issues are addressed promptly

### During Analysis
- [ ] Analysis plan is documented before beginning
- [ ] Data cleaning procedures are transparent
- [ ] Analytical decisions are recorded
- [ ] Multiple analysts verify key findings when possible
- [ ] Alternative explanations are considered
- [ ] Limitations are honestly acknowledged
- [ ] Results are checked for errors

### Before Dissemination
- [ ] Findings are accurately represented
- [ ] Limitations are clearly stated
- [ ] Implications are supported by evidence
- [ ] All sources are properly cited
- [ ] Co-authors have reviewed and approved
- [ ] Ethical obligations to participants are met
- [ ] Target audiences are identified

## Common Research Pitfalls and Solutions

| Pitfall | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Scope creep | Define boundaries early; use change control |
| Confirmation bias | Pre-register; seek disconfirming evidence |
| Inadequate sample | Power analysis before collection |
| Poor documentation | Use templates; document as you go |
| Analysis paralysis | Set decision criteria in advance |
| Writing delays | Schedule regular writing time; draft early |
| Stakeholder misalignment | Communicate early and often |
| Data quality problems | Pilot instruments; monitor continuously |
| Team conflict | Define roles; address issues early |
| Ethical oversights | Consult IRB; maintain reflexivity |

## Key Statistical Concepts Quick Reference

**Effect Size**: Magnitude of difference or relationship, independent of sample size. Report alongside p-values for meaningful interpretation.

**Confidence Interval**: Range of values likely to contain the true population parameter. Provides more information than point estimates alone.

**Statistical Power**: Probability of detecting an effect when one exists. Aim for 80% minimum; calculate before data collection.

**P-value**: Probability of observing data at least as extreme as obtained, assuming null hypothesis is true. Not the probability that the hypothesis is true.

**Type I Error**: Rejecting a true null hypothesis (false positive). Controlled by significance level (alpha).

**Type II Error**: Failing to reject a false null hypothesis (false negative). Related to statistical power.

**Regression to the Mean**: Extreme values tend to be followed by values closer to average. Account for this in pre-post designs.

## Research Ethics Quick Guide

**Core Principles:**
1. **Respect for persons**: Autonomy and informed consent
2. **Beneficence**: Maximize benefits, minimize harms
3. **Justice**: Fair distribution of research benefits and burdens

**Informed Consent Elements:**
- Purpose and procedures
- Risks and benefits
- Confidentiality protections
- Voluntary participation
- Right to withdraw
- Contact information

**Special Populations Requiring Additional Protection:**
- Children and minors
- Prisoners
- Pregnant women
- Persons with cognitive impairments
- Economically disadvantaged persons
- Employees or students of the researcher
