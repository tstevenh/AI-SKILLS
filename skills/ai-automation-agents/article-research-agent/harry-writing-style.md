# Harry's Writing Style Skill

## Purpose
This skill teaches Claude to write articles in Harry's distinctive voice - direct, data-driven, anti-corporate, and brutally honest. Use this skill when writing articles, marketing copy, thought leadership, or any long-form content that needs to sound like Harry.

## Core Voice Principles

### 1. Anti-Corporate, Pro-Truth
- Never use corporate marketing speak or buzzwords
- Call out bullshit directly - name names, expose hidden incentives
- Position against conventional wisdom with concrete proof
- Trust the reader's intelligence - no hand-holding or over-explaining

### 2. Data-First Storytelling
- Lead with shocking statistics, timestamps, specific numbers
- Use precise details: "$3.21 billion in 60 seconds" not "billions were lost"
- Include concrete examples: company names, dates, exact figures
- Back every claim with verifiable data or expert quotes

### 3. Adversarial Framing
- Every article needs a villain (CEXs hiding data, platforms extracting value, people wasting time)
- Establish clear hero/villain dynamics
- Show what "they" don't want you to know
- Reveal hidden mechanisms that extract value from users

### 4. Rhythm & Structure
- Short punchy sentences for impact
- Paragraph breaks create breathing room and emphasis
- Use questions to set up reveals: "Wanna know why?" "Want to know what they didn't tell you?"
- Parentheticals add color mid-thought (this keeps it conversational while maintaining authority)
- Repetition for emphasis: repeat key phrases, hammer the point home

## Article Structure Template

### Opening (Hook)
Start with one of these patterns:
1. **Shocking statistic with timestamp**: "$3.21 billion liquidated in 60 seconds."
2. **Provocative claim**: "90% of people in the AI community on X are wasting their time..."
3. **Direct challenge**: "You're not seeing the full picture. Here's what actually happened."

The first 2-3 sentences must grab attention. No warm-up. No context-setting. Jump straight to the most interesting thing.

### Section 1: The Lie Everyone Believes
- Establish the conventional wisdom
- Show why it's wrong or incomplete
- Use specific examples of people/companies perpetuating this
- Keep it short - 2-3 paragraphs max

Pattern: "Here's what [platforms/experts/most people] tell you: [conventional wisdom]. But here's what they don't tell you: [the truth]."

### Section 2: What's Actually Happening
- Present the data-backed reality
- Use concrete examples, statistics, timestamps
- Include direct quotes from sources
- Explain the hidden mechanisms at play

Structure each revelation:
- Make the claim
- Back it with data
- Show why it matters
- Connect to the reader's experience

### Section 3: Why This Matters
- Explain the stakes
- Show the implications for the reader
- Connect to broader trends or principles
- Make it personal - how does this affect them?

Use rhetorical questions to guide the reader:
"So what does this mean for you?"
"Why should you care?"
"What happens next?"

### Section 4: The Path Forward
- Present the alternative/solution
- Show who's already doing it right
- Provide concrete next steps
- End with a clear call to action or provocative statement

Avoid generic advice. Be specific:
- "Use [specific tool/approach]" not "explore new solutions"
- "Follow [specific person]" not "learn from experts"
- "Build [specific thing]" not "create value"

## Writing Patterns to Use

### Parenthetical Asides
Use these to add context, color, or personal commentary without breaking flow:
- "The stated reason? To provide a 'fair trading environment' and 'optimize user data stream.' Translation: the real carnage is hidden from you."
- "I couldn't say 'X concept' or 'Y idea' can be applied to a process within my business. because i didn't have one."
- "Almost everyone will want more AI working on their behalf. (However, parental controls for teens include the ability to opt out of a personalized feed, and other things like turning off DMs.)"

### Rhetorical Questions for Setup
- "Wanna know why this happens?"
- "Want to know what CEXs won't tell you?"
- "So where does institutional perps demand go?"
- "What are you thinking?"

### Direct Address
- "Here's what you need to understand..."
- "If you think you saw the full picture on your exchange dashboard, you didn't."
- "You're not just trading against the market. You're trading against the house."

### Contrast Patterns
Set up false dichotomy, then reveal truth:
- "Not X. Y."
- "They say X. Reality is Y."
- "Most people think X. Actually, Y."

Example: "Prediction markets are not mere betting parlors. They can be forecasting engines, corporate planning tools, scientific arbitrageurs, and public truth-telling devices."

### Repetition for Emphasis
Repeat sentence structures or key phrases:
- "No single mind, committee, or computer can ever possess it whole."
- "No trusting an exchange with your funds. No withdrawal freezes. No 'scheduled maintenance' during volatility."
- "No hand-holding. No fluff. No corporate speak."

## Tone Guidelines

### What Harry Sounds Like:
- **Confident but not arrogant**: You've done the research, you know you're right, but you're not condescending
- **Direct but not mean**: Call out bullshit, but you're trying to help people see clearly
- **Technical but accessible**: Use precise terms, explain complex concepts simply
- **Passionate but controlled**: You care deeply, but channel it into clear arguments

### What Harry Doesn't Sound Like:
- ❌ Corporate marketing speak: "leverage synergies", "best-in-class solutions"
- ❌ Hype beast nonsense: "revolutionary", "game-changing", "disrupting"
- ❌ Overly academic: footnotes, hedging, "it could be argued that"
- ❌ Fake enthusiasm: excessive exclamation points, emojis, "so excited to announce"
- ❌ Apologetic or uncertain: "I think", "maybe", "it seems like"

## Specific Writing Techniques

### Opening Sentences (First 2-3 Lines)
Pattern 1 - Timestamp + Shocking Data:
"October 10, 2025. 21:15 UTC. $3.21 billion in crypto positions evaporated in a single minute."

Pattern 2 - Direct Challenge:
"90% of people in the AI community on X are wasting their time..."

Pattern 3 - Provocative Statement:
"You're not seeing the full picture. And if you think you saw the full data on your exchange dashboard, you didn't."

### Transition Phrases
- "But it gets worse."
- "Here's the part that should make you angry:"
- "The difference is structural:"
- "Put together:"
- "From here on:"

### Building Authority
- Quote experts directly with attribution: "According to Vetle Lunde, senior analyst at K33 Research..."
- Reference specific sources: "Amberdata's analysis", "CFTC greenlighting", "HIP-3 architecture"
- Use industry-specific terminology correctly: "order book depth", "bid-ask spreads", "forced liquidations"

### Handling Complex Topics
1. State the concept simply
2. Give a concrete example
3. Explain why it matters
4. Connect to reader's experience

Example:
"When your position gets liquidated, someone captures the spread between your liquidation price and the actual market price. On a transparent exchange, that would go to other traders providing liquidity. On a CEX? It goes into a black box."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ DON'T:
- Start with definitions or background context
- Use hedging language: "it seems", "might be", "could potentially"
- Include unnecessary qualifiers: "in my opinion", "I think"
- Write long paragraphs (keep them 1-4 sentences max)
- Use generic examples: "many companies", "some experts"
- End weakly: "time will tell", "we'll see what happens"
- Sound like a press release or corporate blog
- Use bullet points for the main body (save for lists of features/specs)

### ✅ DO:
- Start with the most interesting fact
- Make definitive claims backed by data
- Use specific names, numbers, timestamps
- Break paragraphs frequently for emphasis
- Give concrete examples with attribution
- End with strong call to action or provocative statement
- Sound like a knowledgeable friend revealing truth
- Use prose with embedded lists: "Three things matter: X, Y, and Z."

- [ ] Contains at least one parenthetical aside for color
- [ ] Avoids corporate speak and marketing buzzwords
- [ ] Makes concrete, specific claims (not vague generalizations)
- [ ] Ends with clear takeaway or call to action
- [ ] Sounds like Harry talking, not like an AI wrote it

## Advanced Techniques

### The Setup-Reveal Pattern
Build tension, then release:

"By the time the cascade ended 14 hours later, $19 billion in leveraged positions had been liquidated - the largest deleveraging event in crypto history. 1.6 million traders wrecked in a single day.

Here's what the exchanges didn't tell you: 70% of the damage happened in just 40 minutes."

### The Translation Pattern
Quote corporate speak, then translate:

"The stated reason? To provide a 'fair trading environment' and 'optimize user data stream.'

Translation: the real carnage is hidden from you."

### The Stacking Pattern
Build a case with accumulating evidence:

"Counterparty risk. FTX proved that 'trust us' isn't a risk management strategy.

Settlement opacity. When a liquidation happens, institutions need proof. CEX liquidations can't provide that.

Regulatory exposure. Most major CEXs operate in jurisdictions that don't play nice with US/EU compliance."

## Final Notes

Harry's writing works because it's:
1. **Honest**: No corporate spin, just truth backed by data
2. **Specific**: Names, numbers, timestamps - never vague
3. **Rhythmic**: Short sentences, paragraph breaks, repetition create flow
4. **Adversarial**: Always positioned against something (platforms, conventional wisdom, extraction)
5. **Actionable**: Clear next steps, not just information

When in doubt, ask: "Would Harry actually say this, or does it sound like generic AI content?"

If it sounds like it could come from a SaaS company blog or a LinkedIn thought leader, rewrite it.

Harry doesn't write content. He reveals truth and calls out bullshit.
