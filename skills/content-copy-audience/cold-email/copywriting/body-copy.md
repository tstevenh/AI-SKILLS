# Body Copy

The body of your cold email is where you deliver value and build interest. After the subject line earns the open and the opening line earns attention, the body must earn the reply. This chapter covers everything you need to write compelling cold email body copy.

## Body Copy Fundamentals

### The Job of Body Copy

After the opening line hooks attention, the body must:

1. **Establish relevance**: "This is for me"
2. **Communicate value**: "This could help me"
3. **Build credibility**: "This person/company is legitimate"
4. **Create interest**: "I want to know more"
5. **Lead to action**: "Here's what to do next"

### Body Copy Length Guidelines

**First Email in Sequence**:
- Ideal: 50-125 words
- Maximum: 150 words
- Minimum: 40 words

**Follow-Up Emails**:
- Even shorter: 25-75 words
- Add new value, not more length

**Why Short Works**:
- Respects recipient's time
- Forces clarity
- Better mobile experience
- Higher completion rates
- Less spam trigger risk

### Body Copy Structure

**The Ideal Flow**:
```
Opening Line (personalized hook)
     ↓
Context/Bridge (why you're reaching out)
     ↓
Value Proposition (what you offer)
     ↓
Social Proof (why to believe you)
     ↓
CTA (what to do next)
```

Each element should be 1-2 sentences maximum.

### The F-Pattern Reality

Eye-tracking shows people read emails in an F-pattern:
- Read first line fully
- Scan down left side
- Occasionally read full lines
- Jump to end

**Implications**:
- Front-load important content
- Use short paragraphs (1-2 sentences)
- Left-align key points
- Don't bury important info in the middle

## Problem-Agitate-Solution for Email

### The PAS Framework

Problem-Agitate-Solution is a classic copywriting framework adapted for cold email:

**Problem**: Identify a pain they likely have
**Agitate**: Amplify why it matters
**Solution**: Present your offering as the answer

### PAS in Cold Email

**Traditional PAS** (too long for cold email):
```
Problem: You're probably struggling to get enough qualified meetings.
Agitate: Every day without a solution, you're losing revenue. Your competitors are booking meetings you should be getting. Your team is frustrated. The pressure is mounting.
Solution: Our platform helps companies like yours 3x their meeting rate in 30 days.
```

**Cold Email PAS** (condensed):
```
[Opening line - personalized]

Most sales teams at your stage are booking half the meetings they should be—leaving significant pipeline on the table.

We helped [similar company] solve this exact problem—they went from 20 to 60 meetings/month in 6 weeks.

Worth a quick call to see if we could do the same for you?
```

### PAS Variations

**Problem-focused** (when problem is well-known):
```
Scaling from founder-led sales is brutal—most Series A companies hit a wall.

We specialize in exactly this transition. [Similar company] credit us for getting past it 3 months faster than expected.

Would it be useful to share their playbook?
```

**Solution-focused** (when solution is novel):
```
What if your SDRs could send truly personalized emails at 10x their current volume?

That's what we do—AI that researches and writes emails that actually sound human.

[Similar company] is seeing 40% reply rates. Worth 15 minutes to see how?
```

**Agitate-focused** (when urgency is key):
```
Every week without [solution], you're losing ground to competitors who have it.

The companies winning in [space] have figured this out. The rest are wondering why their numbers are flat.

We can help you join the first group. Interested?
```

### PAS Best Practices

**Do**:
- Use their language for the problem
- Keep agitation brief (1 sentence max)
- Make solution specific and credible
- Connect to CTA naturally

**Don't**:
- Manufacture fake urgency
- Over-agitate (comes across as manipulative)
- Be vague about the solution
- Forget the CTA

## Feature vs. Benefit Copy

### Understanding the Difference

**Features**: What your product/service does
**Benefits**: What it means for them

**Example**:
- Feature: "Our platform has automated email sequences"
- Benefit: "You'll book 3x more meetings without increasing headcount"

### Benefit-First Writing

Cold email should lead with benefits, not features:

**Feature-First** (weak):
```
Our platform includes:
- AI-powered personalization
- 50+ integrations
- Advanced analytics dashboard
```

**Benefit-First** (strong):
```
Companies like yours are booking 3x more meetings without hiring more SDRs.

Our AI handles the personalization that used to take hours—so your team focuses on closing, not prospecting.
```

### The "So What?" Test

For every statement, ask "So what?"

Statement: "We have AI-powered personalization"
So what? → "It writes personalized emails automatically"
So what? → "Your team sends better emails in less time"
So what? → "You book more meetings without more work"

Keep asking until you reach a benefit they actually care about.

### Feature-to-Benefit Translation

| Feature | Benefit |
|---------|---------|
| Automated sequences | Book meetings while you sleep |
| CRM integration | All data in one place, no manual entry |
| AI personalization | Sound human at scale |
| Advanced analytics | Know exactly what's working |
| 24/7 support | Never stuck with a problem |
| 50+ integrations | Works with your existing tools |
| White-label options | Your brand, our tech |

### Benefit Hierarchy

Some benefits matter more than others:

**Tier 1 (Primary)**: Revenue, time, risk
- "Increase revenue by X%"
- "Save X hours per week"
- "Reduce risk of Y"

**Tier 2 (Secondary)**: Efficiency, quality, ease
- "Streamline process"
- "Improve quality"
- "Simplify workflow"

**Tier 3 (Tertiary)**: Features that enable benefits
- "Includes X feature"
- "Offers Y capability"

Lead with Tier 1. Support with Tier 2. Mention Tier 3 only if asked.

## Social Proof Integration

### Why Social Proof Matters

Social proof answers the question: "Why should I believe you?"

Without social proof, your claims are just claims. With social proof, they're validated.

### Types of Social Proof

**Named Customer Examples**:
"We helped [Company Name] achieve [result]"

**Category References**:
"Companies like yours" or "[Industry] leaders"

**Quantified Results**:
"40% average increase in [metric]"

**Logo References**:
"Trusted by Salesforce, HubSpot, and Gong"

**Testimonial Snippets**:
"As [Name] at [Company] said: '[quote]'"

**Third-Party Validation**:
"#1 on G2 for [category]"

### Integrating Social Proof in Body Copy

**Seamless Integration** (preferred):
```
We helped [Similar Company] go from 20 to 60 demos/month in 6 weeks—your situation looks similar.
```

**Explicit Reference**:
```
Companies like Acme, TechCo, and StartupX use us to [benefit].
```

**Result-Focused**:
```
Our average customer sees a 40% increase in reply rates within 30 days.
```

### Social Proof Selection

Choose social proof that's relevant to your prospect:

**Match Criteria**:
- Same industry
- Same company size
- Same role/department
- Same challenge
- Same stage

**Example**:
For a Series A SaaS company, cite another Series A SaaS company—not an enterprise or bootstrapped startup.

### Social Proof Mistakes

**Avoid**:
- Name-dropping without permission
- Citing results you can't back up
- Using irrelevant examples (wrong industry/size)
- Overloading with proof (1-2 examples max)
- Generic claims ("trusted by thousands")

## Case Study Mentions

### Why Case Studies Work

Case studies are the most persuasive form of social proof because they provide:
- Specific company context
- Concrete results
- Relatable story

### Case Study Formula for Cold Email

**The 3-Part Mention**:
1. Similar company
2. Specific challenge
3. Concrete result

**Example**:
```
[Similar Company] was in your exact situation—growing fast but struggling to scale outbound. After implementing our approach, they went from 10 to 35 qualified meetings/month in 8 weeks.
```

### Case Study Depth Levels

**Level 1: Name + Result** (one sentence)
"We helped [Company] increase demo bookings by 40%."

**Level 2: Context + Result** (two sentences)
"[Company] was struggling to scale outbound past 20 meetings/month. After 6 weeks with us, they hit 50—and maintained it."

**Level 3: Full Mini-Story** (three sentences)
"[Company] hired 5 SDRs but couldn't get them productive—2 months in, they were missing targets. We implemented our system, and within 6 weeks, the team was exceeding quota. They've since expanded to 15 reps using the same playbook."

### Case Study Selection Strategy

**Ideal Case Study Matches**:
1. Same industry
2. Same company size
3. Same challenge
4. Same decision-maker title
5. Same stage/situation

**The More Matches, the Better**: "This company is just like mine" is the goal.

### Case Study Best Practices

**Do**:
- Use real company names (with permission)
- Cite specific, quantified results
- Match case study to prospect profile
- Keep it brief in email (offer details on call)

**Don't**:
- Make up case studies
- Exaggerate results
- Use cases from irrelevant industries
- Include too much detail (save for sales call)

## Brevity vs. Detail Balance

### The Brevity Imperative

Cold email is not the place for comprehensive explanation:

**Recipients Are**:
- Busy
- Skeptical
- Scanning, not reading
- On mobile (often)

**Therefore**:
- Every word must earn its place
- Details come later (in call, in follow-up)
- First email is about earning interest, not closing

### What to Include vs. Exclude

**Include**:
- Personalized opening
- One clear value proposition
- One compelling social proof point
- One clear CTA

**Exclude**:
- Company history
- Full feature list
- Multiple value propositions
- Multiple CTAs
- Long explanations
- Attachments

### The Detail Progression

**Email 1**: Spark interest (50-125 words)
- Just enough to earn a reply
- Curiosity over completeness

**Email 2-3**: Add new angles (25-75 words)
- Different perspectives
- New proof points
- Still brief

**Discovery Call**: Provide detail
- Answer questions
- Explore fit
- Share depth

### Editing for Brevity

**Technique 1: Cut by Half**
Write your email, then cut 50% of words.

**Technique 2: Sentence Elimination**
Remove each sentence one by one. If the email still works, it wasn't necessary.

**Technique 3: Word Reduction**
- "in order to" → "to"
- "at this point in time" → "now"
- "I wanted to reach out" → [delete]
- "I think that" → [delete]

**Example Editing**:
```
Before (89 words):
"I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to reach out to you because I noticed that your company has been growing rapidly and I thought that you might be interested in learning more about how we help companies like yours improve their sales processes. We have a platform that uses AI to automate outreach and our customers typically see a 40% increase in meetings booked."

After (47 words):
"Noticed Acme's rapid growth—congrats. Companies scaling this fast usually hit a wall with their sales process around now.

We helped [Similar Company] break through that wall—40% more meetings in their first month.

Worth 15 minutes to see if we could do the same?"
```

## Mobile Optimization

### The Mobile Reality

**Stats**:
- 50%+ of emails first opened on mobile
- Mobile screens show limited content
- Mobile users are even more impatient

### Mobile-Friendly Formatting

**Paragraph Length**:
- Maximum 2-3 lines on mobile
- 1-2 sentences per paragraph
- White space between paragraphs

**Line Length**:
- Keep lines short
- No long, unbroken sentences
- Natural break points

**Links**:
- Make links tappable (not too close together)
- Clear link text
- Avoid multiple links in body

### Mobile Preview Optimization

The mobile inbox shows:
- Sender name
- Subject line
- First ~10-15 words of body

Optimize all three for mobile scanning.

**Example Mobile View**:
```
John Smith
Question about Acme's hiring
Noticed you're building out the sales team—that's exciting but...
```

### Mobile Testing

Before campaigns, test on:
- iPhone (Mail app)
- Android (Gmail app)
- Various screen sizes

Check:
- Readability
- Link tappability
- Image rendering (if any)
- Overall impression

## Value Proposition Frameworks

### The Core Value Proposition

Your value proposition answers: "Why should I care?"

**Strong Value Prop Elements**:
1. Specific outcome
2. Clear timeframe
3. Relevant context
4. Credibility element

**Example**:
"Helping Series A SaaS companies book 3x more demos in 30 days—without hiring more reps."

### Value Proposition Frameworks

**Framework 1: Outcome + Timeframe**
"[Achieve X outcome] in [Y timeframe]"

Examples:
- "Book 3x more meetings in 30 days"
- "Reduce churn by 40% this quarter"
- "Cut data entry time by 80% this week"

**Framework 2: Without the Downside**
"[Achieve X] without [common downside]"

Examples:
- "Scale outbound without hiring more reps"
- "Improve personalization without spending hours researching"
- "Get enterprise-grade security without enterprise-grade complexity"

**Framework 3: Comparison**
"[Like X] but [better in Y way]"

Examples:
- "Like Outreach, but actually affordable for startups"
- "The power of ZoomInfo without the price tag"
- "Enterprise results with startup simplicity"

**Framework 4: Transformation**
"Go from [current state] to [desired state]"

Examples:
- "Go from drowning in manual tasks to automated efficiency"
- "Transform cold outreach from spray-and-pray to targeted precision"
- "Move from guessing to knowing what works"

### Value Proposition Testing

**Test Different Angles**:
- Speed: "Book more meetings, faster"
- Savings: "Cut your prospecting costs in half"
- Quality: "Higher quality meetings, not just more meetings"
- Simplicity: "Finally, outbound that actually works"

Track which resonates most with your audience.

## Tone and Voice

### Tone Spectrum

**Formal** ←————————→ **Casual**

Cold email typically works best in the center to casual range.

### Tone Guidelines

**Too Formal**:
"I am writing to inquire as to whether your organization might benefit from our enterprise solution offerings."

**Too Casual**:
"Yo! Saw your company is crushing it. We should totally chat about making you guys even more awesome!!"

**Just Right**:
"Noticed Acme is growing fast—congrats. Most companies at your stage struggle with [challenge]. We help with exactly that."

### Voice Consistency

Your voice should be consistent:
- Professional but human
- Confident but not arrogant
- Helpful but not desperate
- Direct but respectful

### Read-Aloud Test

Before sending, read your email aloud:
- Does it sound natural?
- Would you say this in conversation?
- Is it smooth or awkward?

If it sounds like marketing copy, rewrite it.

### Avoiding Corporate Speak

**Replace Corporate Speak**:
- "Leverage" → "Use"
- "Utilize" → "Use"
- "Synergy" → "Work together"
- "Circle back" → "Follow up"
- "Touch base" → "Connect"
- "Moving forward" → "From now on"
- "At the end of the day" → [Delete]
- "Reach out" → "Contact" or "Email"

## Email Structure Templates

### Template 1: The Classic

```
[Personalized opening based on research]

[One sentence identifying their likely challenge/goal]

[One sentence explaining how you help + social proof]

[CTA question]

[Sign-off]
```

**Example**:
```
Noticed Acme's team has doubled this quarter—congrats on the growth.

Scaling sales that fast usually means outbound becomes a bottleneck.

We helped [Similar Company] solve exactly that—3x'd their meeting rate in 30 days.

Worth a quick chat to see if we could do the same?

Best,
John
```

### Template 2: The Direct

```
[Direct statement of purpose]

[Brief explanation of value]

[Social proof]

[Direct CTA]

[Sign-off]
```

**Example**:
```
I help Series A companies scale outbound without hiring a huge team.

Our AI handles personalization at scale—most customers see 40% more meetings in month one.

Companies like [Similar1], [Similar2], and [Similar3] use us for exactly this.

Can I show you how it works in 15 minutes?

John
```

### Template 3: The Problem-Solution

```
[Personalized opening]

[Identify specific problem]

[Your solution + result]

[CTA]

[Sign-off]
```

**Example**:
```
Your recent LinkedIn post about SDR productivity really resonated.

Most teams I talk to can't figure out how to make reps more effective without burning them out.

We solved this for [Similar Company]—their reps now handle 2x the volume with better results.

Would it help to see how they did it?

John
```

### Template 4: The Value-First

```
[Offer immediate value]

[Brief context on why]

[Credibility statement]

[Easy CTA]

[Sign-off]
```

**Example**:
```
Put together a quick analysis of Acme's cold email setup—spotted 3 things that could improve your reply rate.

Happy to share it regardless of whether we ever work together.

We do this kind of work for [Similar Companies] all the time.

Want me to send it over?

John
```

## Body Copy Checklist

### Before Sending

- [ ] Total length under 125 words (first email)
- [ ] Clear value proposition
- [ ] At least one social proof element
- [ ] Mobile-friendly formatting
- [ ] No corporate jargon
- [ ] Benefits over features
- [ ] Reads naturally aloud
- [ ] Single clear CTA
- [ ] No typos or grammar errors
- [ ] Personalized to recipient

### Quality Questions

1. Would I keep reading if I received this?
2. Is the value clear within 10 seconds?
3. Do I believe the claims being made?
4. Is the CTA easy to respond to?
5. Does this respect the recipient's time?

---

## Summary: Body Copy Principles

**Brevity Beats Verbosity**: Say more with less.

**Benefits Over Features**: They care what it does for them.

**Social Proof Builds Trust**: Show, don't just tell.

**Structure Matters**: Follow the flow from hook to CTA.

**Mobile First**: Assume small screens and impatient readers.

**Human Voice**: Write like a helpful person, not a marketing robot.

---

*The body copy builds the case for your CTA. Make every word count.*
