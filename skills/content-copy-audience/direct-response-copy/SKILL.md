# Direct Response Copy

Write like a smart operator explaining something valuable to a skeptical friend.

The point is not to sound "salesy." The point is to move the reader to a clear next action.

## When to use this skill

Use it for:

- landing pages
- sales pages and offers
- email campaigns and launches
- ads and promo assets
- onboarding and trial conversion flows
- pricing pages
- product messaging that must drive clicks, replies, bookings, or purchases

Do not use it for:

- pure brand-awareness campaigns
- PR or corporate comms
- generic SEO blog content
- internal documentation

## Core rule

Copy is salesmanship in print:

- clarity beats cleverness
- specificity beats vagueness
- proof beats claims
- one big idea beats five half-ideas

## Before writing: intake

Capture this first:

1. Offer: what is being sold or proposed?
2. Audience: who exactly is this for?
3. Problem: what painful or expensive thing do they want fixed?
4. Desired outcome: what transformation do they want?
5. Proof: what evidence makes the promise believable?
6. Objections: why would they hesitate?
7. CTA: what exact action should they take now?

## Awareness-level match

Match the copy to the reader's awareness.

| Awareness | What they know | What copy should lead with |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Most aware | They know you and the offer | Deal, urgency, CTA |
| Product aware | They know your product | Differentiation and proof |
| Solution aware | They know solutions exist | Unique mechanism and why this works |
| Problem aware | They feel the pain | Problem, stakes, and relief |
| Unaware | They are not looking yet | Identity, story, curiosity |

If you mismatch awareness, the copy feels off. Do not lead with a hard CTA to an audience that still needs education.

## Working structure

Use this sequence for most direct-response work:

1. Research
2. Pick the big idea
3. Match awareness level
4. Draft the headline set
5. Write the lead
6. Build desire with benefits, mechanism, and proof
7. Handle objections
8. Make the CTA obvious
9. Edit for force and rhythm

## Headline rules

Good headlines usually do one or more of these:

- promise a clear result
- sharpen contrast
- introduce curiosity
- show specificity
- call out the reader directly

Useful patterns:

- `How to [result] without [pain]`
- `Get [result] in [timeframe]`
- `From [bad state] to [desired state]`
- `Why [common belief] is wrong`
- `The [specific] guide to [result]`

Write 15-25 headline options before picking one.

## Lead rules

The lead only needs to earn the next sentence.

Strong lead types:

- direct challenge
- vivid result
- confession
- story opening
- sharp question

Weak lead types:

- generic welcome copy
- fake enthusiasm
- empty "in today's world" setup

## Body rules

Move from tension to proof to action.

Use proven frameworks when useful:

- `AIDA` for broad sales flow
- `PAS` for shorter copy and ads
- `PASTOR` for longer-form pages and VSL-style arguments

Across all frameworks, make sure the draft includes:

- the real problem
- consequences of leaving it unsolved
- the mechanism or reason-why
- concrete proof
- objection handling
- a low-friction CTA

## Proof hierarchy

Best proof usually looks like this:

1. concrete outcome with numbers
2. before/after case study
3. demo or screenshot proof
4. testimonial with specific detail
5. authority or third-party validation

If you cannot prove the promise, soften the promise.

## Offer and CTA rules

The CTA should answer:

- what do I do?
- what happens next?
- why now?
- what risk is removed?

Examples:

- `Book the audit`
- `Start the 14-day trial`
- `Get the playbook`
- `Reply with "interested"`

Add urgency only when it is real.

## Editing checklist

- Is the big idea obvious?
- Does the headline sell the next line?
- Is the language concrete and specific?
- Are benefits connected to outcomes?
- Is there enough proof for the promise?
- Are the biggest objections handled?
- Is the CTA singular and unmissable?
- Does anything sound like generic marketing filler?

## Output format

Default output:

1. Audience and awareness summary
2. Big idea
3. 10-15 headline options
4. Final draft
5. CTA options
6. Notes on proof gaps or assumptions
