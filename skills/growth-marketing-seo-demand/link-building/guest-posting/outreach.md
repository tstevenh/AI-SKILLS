# Outreach for Guest Posts

## The Art of Getting "Yes"

Outreach is where link building campaigns live or die. You can find perfect prospects and create amazing content, but if your emails don't get opened and answered, none of it matters.

This chapter covers everything from finding the right contacts to crafting compelling pitches to following up effectively.

## Finding Contact Information

### Where to Look First

**1. Guest Post / Write for Us Pages**
Most sites with guest post pages include submission instructions:
- Contact email
- Submission form
- Editor name
- Guidelines to follow

**2. Contact Pages**
Look for:
- General contact email
- Editorial contact
- Content team contact
- "For press" or "For media" contacts

**3. About Pages**
Often list:
- Team members
- Founders
- Editors/Content managers
- With social profiles or emails

**4. Author Pages/Bios**
Individual contributor pages may show:
- Editor who manages contributors
- Author email addresses
- Social media handles

**5. Footer Information**
Sometimes includes:
- General email
- Legal contact (last resort)
- Social links

### Email Pattern Guessing

If tools don't work, try common patterns:

**Most common formats:**
- first@domain.com
- first.last@domain.com
- firstlast@domain.com
- flast@domain.com (first initial + last)
- first_last@domain.com
- last@domain.com

**Find the pattern:**
1. Look up any employee on Hunter/Snov
2. Identify the email format
3. Apply to your target contact

**Verification:**
- Use Hunter's email verifier
- Use NeverBounce
- Use ZeroBounce
- Send to a disposable test first (risky but works)

### LinkedIn Prospecting

**Finding the right person:**
1. Search "[Company] + Content Manager"
2. Search "[Company] + Editor"
3. Search "[Company] + Head of Content"
4. Look at company page → People section

**Getting their email:**
- Snov.io Chrome extension
- RocketReach Chrome extension  
- ContactOut Chrome extension
- Ask directly via LinkedIn message

### When You Can't Find an Email

**Alternative approaches:**

**Contact forms:**
- Less effective than email
- But better than nothing
- Make subject line compelling

**Social media DMs:**
- Twitter often works well
- LinkedIn for professional outreach
- Instagram for lifestyle/consumer sites

**Generic addresses:**
- info@domain.com
- editor@domain.com
- contact@domain.com
- hello@domain.com
- submissions@domain.com

**Ask to be connected:**
- "Who handles guest post submissions?"
- May be forwarded to right person

## Crafting the Perfect Pitch

### Email Subject Lines

Your subject line determines if your email gets opened. A/B test results consistently show:

**What works:**
- Specific and relevant
- Mentions their site name
- Clear intent
- Moderate length (40-60 characters)

**What doesn't work:**
- "Guest post inquiry" (too generic)
- ALL CAPS ANYTHING
- Clickbait
- Overly long
- No subject (looks like spam)

**Effective subject line formulas:**

**Formula 1: Direct ask**
"Guest post idea for [Site Name]"
"Contribution pitch for [Site Name]"

**Formula 2: Topic teaser**
"[Topic] article idea for your readers"
"Article idea: [Compelling Topic]"

**Formula 3: Social proof**
"Contributor to [Similar Site] - guest post idea"
"[Company/Name] - article submission"

**Formula 4: Question**
"Still accepting guest contributors?"
"Open to [Topic] content?"

**Subject lines that work well:**

1. `Guest post: [Specific Topic Idea] for [Site Name]`
2. `Contribution idea: [Topic] guide for [Site]`
3. `Quick question about guest posts on [Site]`
4. `Article pitch: [Catchy Title]`
5. `[Your Name/Company] guest post submission`

### The Perfect Pitch Structure

**Element 1: Personalized opening (2-3 sentences)**
- Reference something specific about their site
- Show you've actually visited and read content
- Establish relevance immediately

**Element 2: Your pitch (3-4 sentences)**
- Clear article idea with title
- Brief description of what you'll cover
- Why their audience will care

**Element 3: Your credibility (2-3 sentences)**
- Relevant experience or expertise
- Previous publications (if impressive)
- Why you're qualified to write this

**Element 4: Call to action (1-2 sentences)**
- Clear ask
- Make it easy to say yes
- Optional: offer alternatives

### Pitch Length Guidelines

**Ideal length:** 100-200 words

**Why short works:**
- Editors are busy
- Long emails look like spam
- Key info should be scannable
- Shows you respect their time

**What to include:**
- Who you are (brief)
- What you're proposing
- Why you/why now
- Next steps

**What to leave out:**
- Company history
- Life story
- Multiple pitch ideas
- Attachments (usually)

### Personalization That Works

**Levels of personalization:**

**Level 1: Basic (minimum acceptable)**
- Use their name
- Reference their site by name
- Mention general topic area

**Level 2: Standard (what you should do)**
- Reference a specific recent article
- Explain why your idea fits their content
- Show familiarity with their style

**Level 3: Advanced (for important targets)**
- Reference multiple pieces of their content
- Connect your expertise to their gaps
- Demonstrate deep understanding of their audience

**Personalization examples:**

**Bad (no personalization):**
"I'm reaching out about a guest post opportunity..."

**Basic:**
"I'm a regular reader of [Site] and would love to contribute..."

**Standard:**
"I really enjoyed your recent piece on [Topic] - your point about [specific thing] was spot on. Building on that, I'd like to pitch..."

**Advanced:**
"I've been following [Site] since your [specific early article], and I've noticed you've been expanding your [topic area] coverage lately. Given my background in [relevant experience], I think I could add to that with..."

## Email Templates

### Template 1: Standard Guest Post Pitch

**Subject:** Guest post idea for [Site Name]: [Topic]

```
Hi [Name],

I came across your recent post on [topic] and appreciated how you [specific observation]. It's clear you have high standards for your content.

I'd love to contribute to [Site Name] with an article on:

"[Proposed Title]"

The piece would cover:
• [Point 1]
• [Point 2]  
• [Point 3]

I've previously written for [Publication 1] and [Publication 2] on similar topics, so I'm familiar with what quality [industry] content looks like.

Would this be of interest? I'm happy to send over a detailed outline or adjust the angle based on your feedback.

Best,
[Name]
[Title/Company]
```

### Template 2: Topic Suggestion Pitch

**Subject:** Article suggestion for [Site Name]

```
Hi [Name],

I noticed [Site Name] has covered [related topic] but hasn't yet explored [your topic]. Given your audience of [target readers], I think there's an opportunity here.

I'd like to propose:

"[Proposed Title]"

This would be a [length] guide covering [brief overview], with [unique angle or data point].

A bit about me: I'm [brief credential] with [experience]. My work has appeared in [publications if relevant].

Does this sound like something that would fit [Site Name]? I'm flexible on the approach if you have a different angle in mind.

Thanks,
[Name]
```

### Template 3: Expert Contribution Pitch

**Subject:** Expert contribution for [Site Name]

```
Hi [Name],

I'm [Name], [Title] at [Company]. We specialize in [relevant expertise].

"[Proposed Title]"

• I can share [unique insight/data/perspective]
• This addresses [reader pain point]

I'm not looking for promotional content - just a genuinely useful piece for your audience with a brief bio.

Worth exploring?

[Name]
[Title]
[Company]
```

## Follow-Up Sequences

### Why Follow-Up Matters

**Statistics on follow-up:**
- 80% of sales require 5+ follow-ups
- Most people give up after 1-2
- Follow-ups often outperform initial outreach
- Busy editors miss emails all the time

### The Follow-Up Sequence

**Email 1: Initial pitch**
Day 0

**Email 2: Gentle bump**
Day 5-7

```
Hi [Name],

Just checking if you had a chance to see my guest post pitch from last week. Still very interested in contributing to [Site Name].

Let me know if you'd like me to send over more details.

Best,
[Name]
```

**Email 3: Value add**
Day 12-14

```
Hi [Name],

Following up one more time on my guest post pitch. I've since [relevant update - published elsewhere, gathered more data, etc.] which I think strengthens the piece.

Here's the original idea again: "[Title]"

If this isn't a fit, no worries at all - I understand you're busy.

Best,
[Name]
```

**Email 4: Final follow-up**
Day 20-25

```
Hi [Name],

Last follow-up on this - I know you're busy!

If guest posts aren't a priority right now, I totally understand. If they are, I'm still keen to contribute [topic] to [Site Name].

Either way, thanks for your time.

Best,
[Name]
```

### Follow-Up Best Practices

**Do:**
- Keep follow-ups shorter than initial email
- Reference original pitch briefly
- Add new value when possible
- Use friendly, understanding tone
- Stop after 3-4 attempts

**Don't:**
- Send daily follow-ups
- Be passive-aggressive
- Repeat entire original email
- Make them feel guilty
- Burn bridges

### Handling Common Responses

**"Send me more details"**
→ Provide outline, previous work samples, or full draft depending on ask

**"Not right now, check back later"**
→ Add to follow-up calendar for 2-3 months later

**"We don't accept guest posts"**
→ Thank them, remove from list

**"We charge for placement"**
→ Decide based on site quality and budget, negotiate if appropriate

**"We'll publish but it's nofollow"**
→ Evaluate based on other factors (traffic, brand)

**"Your topic doesn't fit"**
→ Ask what topics they are looking for

**No response after 4 emails**
→ Move on, try again in 6 months if site is valuable

## Pitch Personalization at Scale

### The Personalization Challenge

Problem: You need to send hundreds of emails but personalization drives results.

Solution: Systematize personalization with templates and research.

### The Personalization System

**Create a research checklist:**
For each prospect, spend 2-3 minutes finding:
- Editor's first name
- Recent article topic
- One specific observation about their content

**Build a swipe file:**
- 3-5 intro variations
- 3-5 pitch body variations
- 3-5 credibility statements
- Multiple topic ideas

**Use mail merge thoughtfully:**
- Personalize first 1-2 sentences manually
- Template the body
- Customize the topic/angle

## Negotiation Tactics

### Discussing Pricing

**When they name a price:**

If reasonable: Accept and proceed
If high: "That's a bit above our typical budget. Could you do $X?"
If way high: "Thanks for letting me know. That's outside our range - I'll keep you in mind for future."

**When asked for your budget:**

Option 1: State range
"We typically work in the $X-Y range for sites like yours."

Option 2: Ask their rate
"What do you usually charge for contributions?"

Option 3: Trade value
"We could offer [social promotion/backlink/etc.] instead of payment."

### Negotiation Tactics

**Tactic 1: Bundle**
"If we commit to 3 posts, could you offer a better rate?"

**Tactic 2: Value trade**
"What if we included a link to your site in our newsletter?"

**Tactic 3: Lower quality option**
"Do you have any other placements at a lower price point?"

**Tactic 4: Future promise**
"If this goes well, we'd like to do monthly. Would that affect the rate?"

**Tactic 5: Honest constraint**
"Our budget for this campaign is $X. Is there anything we can do at that level?"

### What You Can Negotiate

- Price (obvious)
- Anchor text
- Link placement (bio vs body)
- Number of links
- Publication timeline
- Content requirements
- Follow/nofollow status
- Promotional support

### Know Your Walk-Away Point

Before negotiating, know:
- Maximum you're willing to pay
- Minimum link value you need
- Deal-breakers (nofollow, bad placement, etc.)

Never negotiate against yourself - have limits and stick to them.

## Outreach Metrics and Optimization

### Key Metrics to Track

**Response rate**
Total responses ÷ Emails sent
Benchmark: 8-15%

**Positive response rate**
Interested responses ÷ Emails sent
Benchmark: 3-8%

**Acceptance rate**
Accepted pitches ÷ Positive responses
Benchmark: 40-60%

**Close rate**
Published posts ÷ Emails sent
Benchmark: 2-5%

### A/B Testing

**What to test:**
- Subject lines
- Opening lines
- Topic approaches
- Call-to-action wording
- Follow-up timing

**How to test:**
- Split list randomly
- Test one variable at a time
- Use significant sample sizes (50+ per variant)
- Track results systematically

### Continuous Improvement

**Weekly review:**
- Response rate by template
- Response rate by site type
- Common objections received
- Successful pitches patterns

**Monthly optimization:**
- Update templates based on data
- Refine targeting based on acceptance rates
- Adjust follow-up sequences
- Retire underperforming approaches

## Summary

Successful outreach requires:

1. **Find the right contacts** - Editors, not generic addresses
2. **Craft compelling pitches** - Personalized, concise, valuable
3. **Use proven templates** - But customize for each prospect
4. **Follow up systematically** - Most wins come from follow-up
5. **Negotiate effectively** - Know your value and limits
6. **Track and optimize** - Data-driven improvement

With effective outreach generating acceptances, the next step is creating content that gets published and drives results.
