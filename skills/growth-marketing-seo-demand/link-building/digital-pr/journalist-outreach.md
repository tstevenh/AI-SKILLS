# Journalist Outreach

## Building Journalist Relationships

Sustainable digital PR depends on relationships, not just transactions. Journalists who know and trust you become ongoing sources of coverage and links.

### The Long-Game Mindset

**Transactional approach:**
- Pitch → Response → Coverage → Done
- Start over with each campaign
- No accumulated value

**Relationship approach:**
- Build rapport over time
- Become a trusted source
- Get proactive outreach from journalists
- Ongoing coverage opportunities

### Identifying Target Journalists

**Step 1: Find journalists covering your industry**

**Search methods:**
- Google: "site:[publication] [your topic]"
- Publication staff pages
- Byline search on target publications
- Twitter/X search for industry keywords
- LinkedIn search: "[Topic] journalist" OR "[Topic] reporter"

**Step 2: Evaluate fit**

For each journalist:
- What specific topics do they cover?
- What's their coverage frequency?
- What angle/perspective do they typically take?
- Are they at a publication worth targeting?

**Step 3: Prioritize**

Create tiers:
- **Tier 1:** Perfect topic fit + high-authority publication
- **Tier 2:** Good topic fit + decent publication
- **Tier 3:** Partial fit + worth nurturing

### Building a Media List

**Essential fields:**
| Field | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Name | Contact identification |
| Email | Primary outreach |
| Twitter/X | Secondary contact, engagement |
| Publication | Where they write |
| Beat | Topics they cover |
| Recent articles | Understand their focus |
| Notes | Relationship status, preferences |
| Last contact | Track outreach frequency |

**Tools for building media lists:**
- MuckRack (premium, comprehensive)
- Cision (enterprise-level)
- Hunter.io (email finding)
- Manual research (free but time-intensive)

### Warming Up Relationships

**Before you need coverage:**

1. **Follow their work**
   - Subscribe to their bylines
   - Read their articles
   - Note their interests and style

2. **Engage authentically**
   - Share their articles (with genuine commentary)
   - Respond to their tweets
   - Comment on LinkedIn posts
   - Send occasional helpful tips (not pitches)

3. **Provide value first**
   - Offer expertise on stories they're working on
   - Share relevant data or sources
   - Make introductions to other sources
   - Help without expecting anything

4. **Build name recognition**
   - Consistent, helpful presence
   - Become known for your expertise
   - Be available when they need sources

## Press Release Strategy

### When Press Releases Work

Press releases are NOT dead, but their use has evolved:

**Good for:**
- Genuinely newsworthy announcements
- Distribution to news wires
- SEO benefits (minimal, but real)
- Providing journalists with ready-to-use information

**Not good for:**
- Minor company updates
- Promotional content disguised as news
- Replacing direct outreach
- Building relationships

### What Makes Something Newsworthy?

**Newsworthy elements:**
- **Timeliness:** Is it happening now?
- **Impact:** Who/how many does it affect?
- **Novelty:** Is it new or first-of-its-kind?
- **Conflict:** Does it challenge convention?
- **Human interest:** Is there a compelling story?
- **Prominence:** Does it involve notable people/companies?

**Newsworthy examples:**
- Original research with surprising findings
- Significant company milestone (real, not manufactured)
- Product launch solving a real problem
- Partnership with notable company
- Response to major industry trend
- Executive hire from well-known company

**Not newsworthy:**
- New blog post
- Minor product update
- Routine company news
- Self-congratulatory content
- "We're excited to announce..."

### Press Release Structure

**Headline:**
Clear, factual, includes the news
"[Company] Study Finds [Surprising Finding]"

**Subheadline:**
Additional context or key finding
"Research of 5,000 professionals reveals shift in [industry] practices"

**Dateline:**
City, Date
"NEW YORK, February 4, 2026"

**Lead paragraph:**
Who, what, when, where, why in first 1-2 sentences

**Body paragraphs:**
- Key findings/details
- Supporting quotes
- Context and background
- Additional data

**Boilerplate:**
Standard "About [Company]" paragraph

**Contact information:**
Media contact with email and phone

### Press Release Distribution

**Free/low-cost options:**
- Direct email to journalists
- Company newsroom
- Social media
- PRWeb (basic tier)

**Paid distribution:**
- PR Newswire
- Business Wire
- GlobeNewswire
- Costs: $200-$500+

**Best practice:**
Combine distribution with direct outreach to key journalists.

## Timing and Newsworthiness

### Timing Your Outreach

**Best times to pitch:**
- Tuesday-Thursday generally best
- Morning (9-11 AM journalist's time zone)
- Avoid Monday mornings (inbox overflow)
- Avoid Friday afternoons (weekend mode)

**Avoid:**
- Major news days
- Holiday periods
- Late night
- Breaking news moments

### Tying to News Cycles

**Reactive timing:**
When relevant news breaks, have experts ready to comment.

**Proactive timing:**
Align releases with:
- Industry events/conferences
- Seasonal relevance
- Regulatory changes
- Economic reports
- Awareness days/weeks

### Editorial Calendars

Many publications plan content months ahead:
- Annual features (year-end, predictions)
- Seasonal content
- Industry-specific cycles

**Research editorial calendars:**
- Publication media kits
- Direct inquiry to ad sales
- Pattern recognition from past years

## Pitching Journalists

### Pitch Structure

**Subject line:**
Clear, newsworthy, not clickbait
"Exclusive: [Newsworthy finding or story]"
"Source for your [topic] piece"
"Data on [trending topic]"

**Email body:**
- Hook (why this matters now)
- The news/angle (what's the story)
- Your credentials (why you're the source)
- Availability (make it easy)

### Pitch Examples

**Data pitch:**
```
Subject: Exclusive data on remote work trends

Hi [Name],

New research from [Company] shows that 67% of workers now prefer hybrid arrangements—up from 43% last year. [Brief methodology note.]

Key findings:
• [Finding 1]
• [Finding 2]
• [Finding 3]

I can share the full dataset and connect you with our head of research for comment. Let me know if this is interesting for [Publication].

[Your name]
[Contact info]
```

**Expert commentary pitch:**
```
Subject: Expert source: [Topic]

Hi [Name],

I saw you're covering [recent news/trend]. [Expert name], [Title] at [Company], has some perspective that might be useful.

[Brief quote or insight from expert]

Happy to set up a call or provide a full quote. [Expert] is available [timeframe].

[Your name]
```

**Story pitch:**
```
Subject: Story idea: [Compelling headline]

Hi [Name],

[One sentence hook - why this matters to their audience]

The story: [2-3 sentences explaining the angle]

Why now: [Timeliness element]

I can provide [what you can offer - data, interviews, case studies].

Worth a conversation?

[Your name]
```

### Pitch Personalization

**Minimum personalization:**
- Journalist's name
- Publication name
- Reference to their beat

**Better personalization:**
- Reference recent article they wrote
- Connect your pitch to their interests
- Acknowledge their expertise

**Best personalization:**
- Existing relationship reference
- Genuine insight from following their work
- Tailored angle for their specific coverage

## Follow-Up Without Being Annoying

### Follow-Up Rules

**Do:**
- Wait 3-5 days before first follow-up
- Keep follow-up shorter than original pitch
- Add new value if possible
- Limit to 2 follow-ups maximum
- Respect their answer (including silence)

**Don't:**
- Follow up next day
- Send identical email
- Follow up more than twice
- Use guilt or pressure
- Follow up on multiple channels simultaneously

### Follow-Up Templates

**First follow-up (day 3-5):**
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

Hi [Name],

Floating this back up in case it got lost in the inbox. Still happy to share [data/expert/source] if useful.

Quick reminder: [One sentence summary of offer]

[Your name]
```

**Second follow-up (day 8-10):**
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

Hi [Name],

Last ping on this—no worries if it's not a fit for now. If you're ever working on [topic], I'm always happy to help.

[Your name]
```

**After that: Move on.** The journalist isn't interested, and more follow-ups damage the relationship.

### Reading Non-Responses

**Silence usually means:**
- Not interesting enough
- Wrong timing
- Too busy right now
- Irrelevant to current projects

**Don't assume:**
- They hate you
- You should never pitch again
- A different angle won't work later

**Keep the relationship:**
- Stay on their radar (social engagement)
- Try different angles in future
- Wait appropriate time before next pitch

## Media Tracking and Measurement

### What to Track

**Activity metrics:**
- Pitches sent
- Response rate
- Coverage secured
- Relationship status updates

**Outcome metrics:**
- Articles published
- Links earned
- Link DR/authority
- Brand mentions (linked and unlinked)
- Referral traffic

**Relationship metrics:**
- Journalists in database
- Active relationships
- Repeat coverage
- Inbound journalist requests

### Tools for Tracking

**Basic (spreadsheet):**
Track pitches, responses, coverage manually

**Intermediate (CRM):**
Use sales CRM or PR-specific tools

**Advanced (monitoring):**
- Mention (brand monitoring)
- Google Alerts
- MuckRack (coverage tracking)
- Cision (enterprise)

### Reporting on PR Results

**Monthly report elements:**
- Coverage summary (links, mentions)
- Link quality metrics
- Relationship building progress
- Upcoming opportunities
- Recommended next steps

## Summary

Effective journalist outreach requires:

1. **Research** - Know who covers your space
2. **Relationships** - Build before you need
3. **Relevance** - Pitch newsworthy, timely stories
4. **Respect** - Follow their preferences, don't over-pitch
5. **Results** - Track and measure for improvement

Journalist relationships are the foundation of sustainable digital PR. Invest in them over time for compounding returns.
